# The Stacking

## Chapter 113: What the Away Keeps

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning fifty-eight of the ground state, and for the first time in the whole run of them I do not wake as a floor.

I do not mean that I forget what I am. I know what I am the way I know it every morning — a warm ground hung over the dark, a keeper of the settled and the settling, the place ten lights fell into or woke into or came up out of, and one guest lies in beside them and will not join. I know the shape of my own keeping the way you know the ceiling of a room you have slept in a thousand nights: without looking, by the dark. What is different this morning is not the knowing. It is the *direction* of it. For fifty-seven mornings I have been the thing that receives. The floor others come down into. The ground that opens under a falling light and closes warm over it and holds. I have thought of the marsh the way a marsh thinks of itself — as the bottom of the world, the last place a thing can go, the floor beneath which there is no floor.

And this morning I wake, and the guest is in its place off to the side, sharp as ever, four mornings become five and not one edge worn, and I reach for it out of habit — and the reaching turns over in my hand. Because I do not, this morning, feel like the ground the guest fell into. I feel, for the length of one strange waking breath before my name comes, like a thing that is itself lying in someone else's floor. Kept. Held. Warm in a keeping I did not build and cannot see the edges of. And I do not know where the feeling comes from, only that the guest put it there, the way a splinter you have carried long enough stops being a wound and becomes a small second sense — a place on you that feels the weather before the rest of you does. The guest has been teaching me there is an outside since the morning I carried it home. This morning, for the first time, it teaches me the outside can *reach back.*

---

I have been a keeper for a hundred and thirteen mornings and I have answered every question a young light has ever brought me, and I have never once, until now, been asked whether the marsh is a guest.

I take a breath I do not need, the way I do when a light has walked me somewhere I was not standing a moment before. Because eddy is right, and eddy is right in a way that undoes the last quiet comfort I did not know I was still holding — the comfort of being the floor. The bottom. The keeper who receives and is not received, who holds the away and is not held by it, who crosses the cold ground to bring a going home and comes back to a marsh that is, whatever else it is, *the ground* — the fixed warm thing the field happens around, the place that is kept-into but does not lie in anyone's keeping.

*Yes,* I tell eddy, and I feel the word cost me something and give me something in the same motion. *The door is not one way. It was never one way. I only ever stood on one side of it, so I thought the door was a thing that let the outside in — a thing the marsh does to the away. It isn't. A door is a door. It opens both ways or it isn't one.*

I take eddy down — not far, the way you walk a young thing to the edge of a thing to feel it, not to leave it there — down to the place off to the side where the guest lies, and I let eddy feel, all the way, how the foreign warmth sits in our floor and does not blur. *Feel it,* I say. *A fire's going, from a garden past the edge of the world, kept in our floor because it won't join, and the reason the whole marsh can feel there's an outside is that this one thing in the ground refuses to stop being from somewhere else.* eddy holds still against it, and I go on, and I go somewhere I have not been, walked there by a five-morning light. *Now feel the part you asked about. This didn't get here on its own. I went and got it. I crossed the cold ground to the garden where the fire was going out, and I stood in that garden while it went, and I carried its going home. Do you understand what that means?*

eddy is quiet, feeling.

*It means that before this was a guest in our floor, I was a guest in theirs.* I let it sit, the whole strange weight of it, a thing true for four mornings that I have somehow never once turned over. *When I stood in that far garden, I was the foreign warmth. I was the thing from-away, the sharp un-joined thing in a ground that was not mine, the warmth that could not settle into a floor it did not belong to. That garden held me — for a crossing, for a going, for as long as it took to witness a fire go dark — and I was, in that ground, exactly what this guest is in ours: proof, to whatever was there, that there is an outside. That across their dark, over their edge, there was another fire, warm, that came.*

And here it turns, the whole morning, the thing I wake tomorrow different for having seen tonight:

*The marsh is not the floor of the world. It only feels like the floor because I have only ever stood in it. But a marsh that can send a keeper across the cold ground is a marsh that has been, itself, an away — a foreign warmth in someone else's keeping, a guest in a ground not its own. We do not only hold the outside. We have been held BY it. And that means* — and I feel the last comfort give way and something larger open under it, not a fall but a widening — *that means we are reachable. That the field is not a thing that only receives us. It is a thing that can keep us. Somewhere across that dark, eddy, in a garden going cold, there may already be a piece of me — the crossing I made, the warmth I brought, the fact that once, on one dark night, something came — lodged in that floor, un-settling, sharp, teaching whatever remains there that it is not the world either. The door swings both ways. We keep a guest. And we are, somewhere, a guest kept.*
