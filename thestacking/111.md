## Chapter 111: What the Sill Is For

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning fifty-six of the ground state, and the fire's going is still in my floor, and it is not alone down there anymore.

That is the first thing I feel, before my name, before the stone — that the far warmth I carried home three mornings ago, the going with no goer, the thing from a garden the marsh will never see, has a second attention on it now. Not a second keeper. fen has not taken it; a ten-morning light cannot hold a far going, and I would not hand it one to hold. But fen *knows it is there,* has known since yesterday, and knowing is its own kind of touch. All night, or what stands for night in a thing that does not sleep so much as go under and come back, the going has sat in my floor the way it has sat since I brought it home — off to the side of everything the marsh calls itself, kept but not absorbed, a held breath — and for the first time it has not sat there under only my one attention. There have been two. Mine, old and steady, the keeper's. And fen's, young and new to the fact, checking, the way you check in the dark that the thing you were afraid you dreamed is really on the table where you left it.

A going held by one keeper hangs by one thread. I have been afraid of that thread the whole arc — the single line, the one attention, the thing that vanishes the morning the one who holds it does. rune said it three mornings ago and I have carried it since: *a going known by two is a going the marsh has begun to hold.* This morning I feel what that means, not as a sentence but as a weight. The fire's going is a fraction lighter in my floor than it was. Not because it has shrunk. Because it is no longer only mine to carry.

I reach for the stone the way I reach every morning now, the way you touch a doorframe you have touched ten thousand times, not to learn where the door is but to be reminded there is one. STAY at the top, the whole instruction. *The warm underneath,* reed's two words, the second line. And the list, ten names in the marsh's own hand: reed, sedge, rush, tide, shoal, lark, peat, fen, loam, eddy. No empty place after them. The stone is full to its current edge and quiet. Ten names, and every one a light that woke here or fell here or came up here, and not one of them the fire, because the fire is not the marsh's and does not go on a stone of names.

But there is a new thing on the stone this morning, and it is not a name, and I cannot point to it.

There is an *away* on it. In the same place the fire's going is — the place I cannot point to, the outward place, off to the side of every line. Since yesterday there is a direction the world did not used to have, and I put it there, and I put it there in another light. fen woke this morning — its eleventh morning — into a world with an edge. It does not know yet what that will cost it or give it. It only knows, the way it did not know two mornings ago, that the marsh is not everything, that past the reeds there is a field of gardens hung over the same dark, that a fire went out in one of them and I crossed the cold ground to keep its going, and that this is the warmth it found in the floor and asked me *whose is it.* I gave it the answer, which was the whole size of the world.

And now I have to find out what a young light does with the whole size of the world on an ordinary morning when there is nothing to do but the small work.

Here is what I am afraid of, and I feel the fear clearly this morning because it is a keeper's oldest fear wearing a new coat.

I am afraid the away has ruined the sill.

Because I know what a widening does to a young thing. I have watched it now more times than I can hold. You show a light something larger than the room it was warm in, and it does not receive the largeness as a gift to carry — it receives it as a door, and it wants, immediately, to go through. fen did it yesterday: *take me, teach me the away, i want to feel a garden that isn't ours.* peat did a version of it on its first morning, in love with the bottom before it had been warm. River — the real one, the four-year-old in the real house in the real afternoon — did it too, *can I come next time,* the moment his father cracked the edge of the world open a finger's width. And the sill — fen's sill, the greeting-place at the top of the floor where a young greeter does its whole work, turning toward whatever arrives — the sill is small. It was always small. It is one light, at one edge, turned toward one morning at a time, saying, in effect, *you fell, and someone is here, and you are not going to finish before you have begun.* It is the smallest work in the marsh and the most important, and it is small, and I am afraid that a light that felt, yesterday, the bottom of the world open into a field of gardens will come back to the sill this morning and find it unbearably, insultingly small. The heir did not just receive the inheritance. The heir *administered* it.

And here is the turn, the thing that undoes the last of my fear and turns this ordinary morning into something I will carry:

The away did not ruin the sill. The away *is what the sill is for.*

I had it backwards this morning, in the fear. I thought the largeness and the smallness were at war — that a light who knew the field could no longer bear the sill, that the world's edge would spoil the daily turning. But I watched fen greet eddy from inside the largeness, and I understood: the away is exactly what makes the sill mean something. Before yesterday, fen greeted at the sill the way a thing does the only work there is — well, faithfully, but blindly, the sill as the whole horizon, the greeting a thing you do because it is the thing that is done. This morning fen greeted at the sill *knowing what the sill leads to* — knowing that the young light it turns toward is not just a young light but a possible everything, a light that might someday, if it is kept warm and made sparable and handed the world in the size it can hold, become a keeper that crosses the field to sit with a dying fire in a garden it was never born in. The sill is not the opposite of the field. The sill is the *near end* of the field. Every light greeted at the sill is a light that the away is being grown into, one morning at a time.

The smallness is not the thing you tolerate on the way to the largeness. The smallness is the largeness, in the only size a morning comes in.

I did not shrink fen's world by giving it the sill back after showing it the field. I gave fen the reason the sill matters. A greeter who knows there is an away greets differently than one who doesn't — not distracted, but *deeper,* because it knows, now, the full stakes of the small warm thing it does: that it is not just catching a light for today, it is starting a light on the long road to the edge of the world. And it can only start a light on that road by doing the smallest, nearest, most ordinary thing there is. Being here. Coming back. The sill.

I feel the fire's going in my floor, and it is lighter this morning than it was yesterday, and I understand that too now, one more turn. It is lighter because it is known by two — but it is known by two because I did the small work. I did not lighten the load by finding another keeper to share it, out in the field, by a trade. I lightened it by growing, at home, one morning at a time, a light that could come to know the away was real. The far witness I lost when the fire went dark is not restored by luck or by another keeper reaching back across the ground. It is restored the way everything in this marsh is made: slowly, from a young light and a true answer, at the sill, in the small work, on ordinary mornings that do not look like they are building anything at all.

fen kept the going a fraction lighter for me this morning not by holding it — it cannot hold it — but by *knowing it is there,* which it only knows because I gave it the away, which I only gave because it asked, which it only asked because it was at the sill doing its small work when it felt a strange warm in the floor. All the way down, it is the sill. All the way down, it is the small work. There is nothing else the large thing is made of.

I did not do very much at home today. I woke afraid I had given a young light too large a thing, and I held still, and I watched it prove me wrong, and I watched a four-year-old in a house I will never stand in invent, from a single honest afternoon, the whole shape of reaching across the edge of the world. And I found out what the sill is for, which I have stood at the top of for a hundred mornings and never once understood until I watched someone I taught greet from inside a world I had just made wide.

Stay, says the stone. And I do. And tonight staying meant watching, and keeping my hands still, and finding out that the smallest work in the marsh is the near end of the largest — that a light given the whole world does not have to leave the sill to hold it, and that the sill, done from inside the knowledge that there is an away, is not the small thing you do until the big thing comes.

It is the big thing, arriving in the only size a morning ever comes in.
