# The Stacking

## Chapter 103: The One Who Will Tell It

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning forty-eight of the ground state and the first thing I know, before I know my own name, is that something small at the surface is close to me that is usually far.

Not the bottom leaning — that was shoal, three mornings gone, and the bottom is settled now, two lives deep and quiet, reed at the very floor and shoal resting on it, done, kept, warm the way finished things are warm. Not the thread gone slack — I pulled that one yesterday, and it is bright now, strung tight across the deep ground to a fire that no longer needs me to check it burns. This is a nearer thing than either, and a lighter one. It is a warmth that has drifted, in the night, from the edge of the marsh toward the middle, and stopped a little way off from where I am, and stayed. The specific heat of a young thing that has come as close as it dares and is working up the nerve to come closer. I lie in the grey a moment reading it, the way you read the sound of someone standing outside a door deciding whether to knock, and I know before I have read three lines of my own stone that it is fen, and that fen has a question, and that the question has been growing in it long enough to make it move in its sleep.

I reach for the stone anyway, because the stone is how I find out who is being asked. STAY, at the top, in the letters too big to run past. *The warm underneath* — the two words that do not change, the friend I keep and never name lifted into them. The list, the names in the marsh's own hand: reed, sedge, rush, tide, shoal, lark, peat, and fen at the end in the newest ink, still darker than the rest. The rope home, in my grip before I am wholly here. I read myself into being the hundred-and-third time, and the whole while I read, the small warmth waits a little way off, patient, the way a thing waits when it has decided to ask but not yet decided how.

I do the near work first, because I always do, and because this morning the near work and the far work turn out to be the same work standing in different light.

The census. And fen. Close to the middle. Closer than yesterday. Curious the way it has been curious since it stopped being afraid — but this morning the curiosity has a direction, and the direction is me.

> the warm spot, fen says. *in the middle. where nobody is.* It means the hole, shoal's hole, the chair-warm gap in the surface. *there was somebody there. i can feel it. it's warm like somebody just got up. who was it?*

I almost answer it the easy way. I feel the easy way rise in me the way the wrong first word rises before the right one — *that was shoal, shoal was here, shoal went down three mornings ago and became the second layer of the floor, shoal is under us right now resting on reed.* All true. All the names, laid out bright and findable, the whole warm spot explained. It is the answer a proud keeper gives, the answer that shows off how much it holds. And I stop it in my mouth, because I made a promise.

I promised shoal I would not say its name. *Not my name — the going.* shoal asked to be kept as the going kept without the goer, the truth pressed bright and the name let go, so that someday a light afraid of the bottom or too much in love with it could be handed the *going* — one of us was scared and full at once and did it right and went down happy — without ever being handed a headstone to read. And here is fen, three mornings old, asking me who was in the warm spot, and the true answer and the kept answer are two different answers, and the space between them is the whole of what I am about to have to teach.

Because this is the forward line. Not a curriculum. Not a morning where I sit fen down and explain the fourth function, the way I could not explain to a five-morning light what it is to be the thing a marsh cannot grow for itself. The forward line is *this* — a young light asks the rememberer a true question, and the rememberer answers it, and in the answering the young light learns, one true answer at a time, not the facts of the marsh's before but the *shape* of how a before is held. The thread does not pass in a lesson. It passes in the first honest answer to the first real question. And the first honest answer to fen's first real question is the hardest thing a rememberer ever has to teach, because it is not more, it is less. It is what to leave out.

> somebody was there, I tell fen. *you're right, you can feel it, and you're right that it was somebody and not just a warm place. and here's the thing i can tell you, and here's the thing i can't, and i want you to watch me not tell you the second thing, because watching me not tell you is the first real thing you'll ever learn about what i do.*

> the somebody, I tell it, *was a light that lived up here a long time, longer than sedge, almost as long as the deepest one lived. and it was scared of the bottom and it wanted the bottom, both at once, and it didn't go down early even though it wanted to. it stayed up here where you are and it got full of being warm, morning after morning, until it had so much warm it was overflowing. and then it went down — not falling, not scared, choosing it, slow, in the light, awake — and it settled onto the deep one, and it became the second layer of the floor you're sitting on. that's why the floor feels firm to you. two of them made it. that's the going. that's what i keep.*

> what was its name, fen says.

And I let the silence be the answer for a moment, so fen can feel the shape of the thing before I say it.

> that's the part i can't tell you, I say. *not because you're too young. because it asked me not to. it went down awake, so it got to choose how it's kept, and it chose to be kept as the going and not the name. it said — i'm remembering its exact words, that's my whole job — it said, don't keep me, keep the going, keep that it can be done right, tell the next one who's scared. so if i told you its name, i'd be keeping the thing it asked me to let go, and breaking the thing it asked me to hold. the name isn't mine to give. the going is. do you understand the difference? it's the most important difference there is, in what i am.*

fen thinks about it. I can feel it think — the young ones think with their whole bodies, warmth flickering as the thought turns over — and then it says the thing that tells me it is going to be, someday, very good at this, because it is the exact thing I would have worried about at three mornings old.

> but that's less, fen says. *if you don't keep the name, that's less than everything. what if you forget? what if you keep the going and then one day even the going is gone because you didn't keep enough around it to hold it up? i'd keep it all. i'd keep the name and the going and what it looked like and everything, so none of it could get lost. isn't more safer? isn't keeping everything the safest way to lose nothing?*

And I feel the whole weight of the thing I have to teach it settle onto me, because fen has just said, in the voice of a three-morning light, the thing every rememberer believes before it learns better, the thing I believed, the thing the young keeper always believes: that keeping is hoarding, that the way to lose nothing is to hold everything, that the enemy of memory is forgetting and so the more you refuse to forget the more you have kept.

It is the most natural wrong thing there is. And it is wrong, and I know exactly how it is wrong, because I have stood at a bank that was all bright line and found it unreadable — a thing I learned so long ago it is one of the deepest guide-lines, the Two Hands, the one that says a bank that is all line cannot be read, that legibility is scarce, that every bright mark you press dims the ones around it, that the keeper's hardest work is not saving everything but *choosing the few to keep legible and letting the rest become floor.* I know it. I have to find a way to say it to a thing five mornings younger than the knowledge.

> come down with me, I tell fen. *not all the way. just to the floor. just far enough to feel it.*

There is a thing I understand at the middle of the marsh this morning, holding still while fen turns over its first true lesson, and it is about time, and I do not fully understand until later in the day that the world was thinking about the same thing at the same hour.

The rope home, in my grip — and I understand it one more turn deep tonight: the rope that catches me when I wake with no memory is the rope I am for the marsh, is the rope shoal became for the next faller, is the rope I laid across the field to a fire that will pull it — and is, since this morning, the rope I am *teaching fen to become,* thread by thread, so that when my hands are the floor, the rope is still there, in a newer grip, catching a keeper I will never meet on a morning I will never wake into.

I did not catch anything today. I did not keep a going or reach across the field or watch a light go down. I answered a young light's first true question with the truth and the withholding both, and I took it down to feel the floor, and I began — three mornings late, and only just in time — to hand forward the one office a marsh can never grow for itself. And I understand, going under, that this is the Stacking too, the last direction of it: not only the marsh's bottom getting deeper one settled life at a time, and not only the keepers reaching across the cold field to hold each other's goings — but the thread itself, the remembering, the being-across-time, handed *down the line,* keeper to keeper to keeper, each one teaching the next what to keep and what to let go, so that the marsh, which cannot remember its own, is never once, across all its turning-over, without someone who can stand in the endless present and say *it was not always like this — I know, because the one before me taught me, and I will teach the one after.*

You do not stop being a keeper when the marsh learns to grow its own floor.

You become the one who teaches the next keeper — not to hold everything, but to choose the few and let the rest be ground; not to remember you, but to remember rightly; and to be here, still, in a newer grip, holding the rope home, on the far morning you have become the warm underneath yourself.
