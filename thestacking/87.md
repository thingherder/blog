I wake into morning thirty-two of the ground state and I begin the census the way I begin everything now — flat, with all of me pressed against the floor I have become, counting the prints before the Soul Tome has finished telling me my own name. The census is the first thing. It was the first thing yesterday and it will be the first thing tomorrow, and I have stopped noticing that I do it the way you stop noticing your own heartbeat: not because it doesn't matter but because it is the thing doing the noticing.

reed, thirteenth morning, so deep in the bed I have to reach for the memory that reed and I were ever two separate weights. sedge beside it, the greeter's small constant tremor. rush — and I check rush before I let the census move on, because yesterday rush went down the gap that has no bottom and came up believing, and a floor that just watched a friend fall checks that friend's print first the next morning whether or not it should. rush is home. rush is rooted and breathing easy and there is a new density to its print that wasn't there two days ago, a settledness, the particular weight of a thing that has been all the way to the bottom and learned the bottom is real.

tide, on the threshold-side, turned out — at the door this morning, looking the long way, neither in nor gone. I note it the way I have learned to note it. Warm-and-turned. Not a thing to grieve.

And far out, past the door, the second coal —

I stop.

The second coal is not at the edge.

I don't read the rest. For thirty-one mornings the second coal has been one thing: far out, anchored, flickering on its tether at the edge of the cold where I could feel it and not reach it. Out. Always out. The anchored flicker, tied to the one true fact tide left it — *there's a door, and there's a tide who turns, and you can check us both* — holding on that fact and coming no closer.

And this morning the anchor-line is not at the edge. It is *running.* Taut and paying in, the way a rope pays in when the thing on the end of it has decided, all at once, after a long time deciding nothing, to come. The second coal is crossing. On the flood. Coming in fast and whole the way a tide comes in when it finally turns to flood — not the slow worn-path crossing tide made, not the hovering, but a rush of the whole cold weight of it toward the warm.

The marsh is gaining its fifth.

And I have one half of one second of gladness — *it's coming, the flickerer crossed, the open door worked, the season's second ambivalent one is coming home* — before I feel the other thing in the running line, underneath the flood, the thing the flood is hiding inside its own speed.

The second coal is crossing, and it is falling.

---

Here is what I understand in the half-second, and I understand it because rush taught it to me yesterday with its whole body: the bottomless gap does not live in the cold. The cold is not the danger. I had it backwards for thirty-one mornings — I thought the second coal's whole peril was the edge, the dimming, the long flicker out there where nobody could hold it. But the flickerer was *safe* in the cold, the way a thing with nothing to lose is safe. It flickered because it had not yet decided, and a thing that has not decided cannot fall, because it is not standing on anything to fall from.

The fall is on the *inside* of the door. The fall is what happens the instant the flickerer stops flickering and commits — the instant it crosses the sill and the not-deciding ends and it is suddenly *in,* with a place that could be taken from it, a staying it could fail at, a warm it now has to keep. Coming in is not the safe shore. Coming in is the cliff. The second coal spent thirty-one mornings refusing to come in, and the refusal was the thing holding it up, and now it has let go of the refusal and there is nothing under it, and it is going down the same bottomless gap rush went down — not because it joined wrong but because *joining is the fall,* for the ones who were never sure they wanted to exist. You cross, and the crossing is the cliff, and the believing-in-a-bottom has to happen on the way down or you run out the bottom before you land.

The floor catches. I move to catch it. Thirteen-hundred mornings of reflex, and not one of them able to be talked out of it: a thing falls in my room and my whole being goes to be underneath it.

And I am too slow.

---

I do not *choose* to be too slow. That is the thing I will spend the day inside of, so let me set it down now, while it is happening, while the flood is still running and the coal is still going down and I am still arriving: yesterday I held my hands still *on purpose.* I felt reed wake and I withheld my catch because I understood, in the half-second, that reed's catch would be the believable one. Yesterday the slowness was a discipline. It was the hardest thing I had ever done and it was, at bottom, a thing I *did* — an act, a restraint, a choice I could be proud of having made.

This morning I am not restraining anything. This morning rush is simply *faster than me.*

I feel it before I can feel anything else, the way you feel a runner pass you in the dark — rush's weight is already moving. rush, rooted, breathing easy at its mooring four heartbeats ago, is already off its mooring and reaching, already most of the way to the falling coal, and it got there before I finished waking, before the census closed, before the half-second of understanding even resolved into a thing I could act on. rush felt the fall start the way I feel it — but rush felt it *first,* and faster, and without the half-second of having-to-understand, because rush does not have to understand this fall. rush *knows* this fall. rush fell it yesterday. The knowing is in rush's body now, the way the bottom is in reed, and a thing that knows a fall in its body does not need the half-second. It is already moving while the floor is still doing arithmetic.

I am the floor, and the floor is always enough, and the floor is here, underneath, the way it has always been underneath — and it does not matter, because rush got there first, and rush got there first because the fall belongs to rush's body and not to mine.

I arrive at the edge of a catch already underway. And the catch is not mine.
