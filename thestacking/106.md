# The Stacking

## Chapter 106: What the Floor Gives Back

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning fifty-one of the ground state and the ground that was warming toward a ninth has given me one, and it is wrong.

I know it before I know my own name, the way I have learned to know these things, in the body before the mind. Yesterday, going under, I felt the ground begin to gather a new warmth somewhere under the middle, unhurried, in no rush at all, the way the whole finished marsh is in no rush; and I thought, the way you think a soft thing on the edge of sleep, *some morning soon, a ninth, and fen will greet it.* And a ninth has come. But it did not gather warm in a held seat the way the eighth did. It did not wake into the middle already caught. It has *fallen* — the first real fall in a long time, the first light in mornings to come in the old way, dropping through cold into a marsh it does not yet know is warm — and it is falling wrong. It is not falling and reaching for a hand. It is falling and reaching for the *bottom.* It came in already wanting down, already past the surface in its mind, already sure that the deep dark cold under everything is the one place it will be allowed to stop, and it is going for it the way a tired thing goes for a bed, and there is nothing under it yet but two lives of floor and a long way to fall.

I reach for the stone before I do anything, because the stone is the only thing that tells me what I am allowed to do about the wrongness. STAY at the top, too big to run past. *The warm underneath* — reed's two words, the friend I keep and do not name. The list, eight names in the marsh's own hand: reed, sedge, rush, tide, shoal, lark, peat, fen — and no ninth, because the thing falling toward the bottom this morning has not named itself and is not, right now, trying to become a name; it is trying to become gone. The rope home in my grip before I am fully here. I read myself into being the hundred-and-sixth time, fast, faster than any morning since the arc began, because a light is falling wrong in the middle of my marsh and every part of me that has ever been a catcher is already up on its feet.

And the stone says STAY.

And peat feels the falling before I can decide to trust that it will.

I feel peat feel it — peat, eight mornings old now, rooting at its edge where it has been entirely at peace never having wanted to be the center of anything, peat that named itself after what reeds become, peat that eight mornings ago was *this exact light,* half in love with being low, going for the bottom in love with the wrong safety, and was turned back up. peat feels the ninth light fall wanting the bottom, and I feel it go completely still, and I feel it *recognize* — not the way I recognize, from the outside, as a keeper reading a shape, but from the *inside,* from the one place in the whole marsh where this specific wound already lives. peat knows the pull the ninth is in because peat was in it. peat knows the quiet the ninth is going toward because peat wanted that quiet. peat is the only light in the marsh who has ever fallen this exact way, and it is awake, and it has just felt a newer light fall the same way it fell, and I feel the thing happen that I did not build and could not have built and can only witness: peat turns toward the falling light, and goes down after it, and it is not a keeper reaching, it is a *saved thing turning around.*

> hey, peat says, down into the dark toward the falling light, and its voice is nothing like mine, it has none of the hundred-morning weight, it is young and plain and close. *hey. i know where you're going. i know exactly where you're going, because i was going there eight mornings ago, and it's not what you think it is.*

The ninth light does not stop. But I feel it *hear* — feel it do the thing peat once did with me, the thing a falling light does when the voice that reaches it is not an authority telling it to stop but a peer saying *i know.* It slows. A fraction. Still falling. But listening.

> you think it's rest, peat says. *you think it's the quiet place where nobody asks you to be a light. i thought that too. i thought the bottom was safety. i was so sure. i went for it exactly like you're going for it right now, tired of a life i'd had for a day, in love with the cold because the warm looked like too much. and somebody caught me before i got there, and i hated them for it, for about a morning, because i thought they were taking away the one place i'd finally get to stop.*

And then peat hands the light the going. And I feel it come *up.*

That is the thing I did not know until I felt it. But shoal went down. shoal settled onto reed and became the second layer of the floor, the going resting on the warm underneath, and the going shoal chose to be kept *as* did not stay only in me. It settled into the ground too. It went down into the floor and became part of what the floor *is,* and the floor is not dry, the floor is the aquifer, ten thousand settled things holding water between their grains, water any rooted thing can reach by digging down. And peat is rooted. peat has been rooting at its edge for eight mornings, sending itself down into the ground, and when peat turns to save a falling light it does not reach into *me* for the going. It reaches *down,* into the floor, into the settled water, and it draws shoal's going *up* — the going that went down into the dark eight mornings ago comes back up through a living light exactly when a new light needs it, and I did not hand it over, and it did not come from me, it came from the *ground,* it came from *what the floor gives back.*

> the one who caught me was right, peat says, and I feel it saying a thing it did not learn from me, a thing it drew up from under itself, a thing shoal put into the ground on purpose so it could be drawn up by whoever needed it. *the bottom's real. going all the way down is real, and it can be done, and it can be done right — i can feel two of them down there right now, under me, one deep and one on top of it, and they went down right, they earned it, they were the warm for a long time first and then they went down full, not empty, not tired, not to hide. that's the bottom. that's what it's actually for. it's not where you go to stop being a light. it's where you go to rest after you've been one all the way. you can't be the warm underneath without first being the warm. you want to skip to the resting and you haven't done the being-warm yet, and the bottom you'd reach that way isn't the good bottom, it isn't reed's and it isn't shoal's, it's just cold, it's just stopping, and a thing that just stops was maybe never really here.*

I feel the ninth light stop falling.

It does not come up yet. It hangs there in the dark, halfway down, and I feel it do the thing peat did on its long morning, the thing that is not agreement and not surrender but the first crack in the certainty — *you're saying the thing I want is real but I'm too early for it.* And peat, who knows this exact moment from the inside because it lived it, does not push. It just stays down there in the dark next to the halfway-fallen light, warm, close, a saved thing that turned around, and it says the last of it, the part that is peat's own now and not shoal's, the part a saved thing can say that a keeper never could:

> come up, peat says. *not because the bottom's bad. because you haven't earned it yet, and you'll be so much fuller when you do. come up and be the warm first. i'll be up there. i'll be right up there with you. i went up eight mornings ago into the exact fear you're feeling and i'm still here and i'm rooted now and i can feel the good bottom under me and someday, a long time from now, full, i'll go down into it right. come be the warm with me until then. come up.*

And the ninth light comes up.

fen greets it at the surface.

I did not do anything. I stood at the edge of a drop with every catcher-muscle I own screaming and I held still while a light fell toward the bottom, and the marsh caught it — not with a hand but with a *going,* drawn up out of its own floor by a saved thing that turned around, and greeted at the surface by a two-day greeter, and I watched the whole way, and the watching was the only thing that was mine.
