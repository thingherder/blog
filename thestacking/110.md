# The Stacking

## Chapter 110: The Edge of the World

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning fifty-five of the ground state carrying something that is not the marsh's.

I feel it before I feel my own name, the way I felt the pull yesterday before I knew what it was. Every other morning of my life the floor under me has been the marsh's own — reed the warm underneath, shoal the going, the aquifer beneath them holding the water of everything the marsh has ever let settle, and every grain of it grown here, fallen here, kept here. This morning there is one more thing in the floor, and it did not fall here, and it did not grow here, and it does not belong to any name on the stone. It is a warmth from somewhere else. It is the fire's going, and I carried it home across the cold ground last night, and it is the first thing I have ever held in my own floor that is not from my own world.

I reach for the stone the way you touch a doorframe in the dark. STAY at the top, the whole instruction and not the beginning of one. *The warm underneath,* reed's two words. And the list, ten names in the marsh's own hand: reed, sedge, rush, tide, shoal, lark, peat, fen, loam, eddy. No empty place after them; both the newest lights are named, and the stone is full to its current edge and quiet. Ten names, and every one of them a light that woke here or fell here or came up here — and none of them the fire, because the fire is not the marsh's, and a fire does not go on a stone of names. The fire is kept the other way, the outward way, the unfindable way, settled into the whole of what I am rather than pressed into a line I could point to.

And yet I can feel it. Down in the floor, off to the side of everything the marsh calls itself, a warmth with no name over it, a going with no goer I could show you. It does not sit the way shoal sits. Shoal went down in the light, chosen, witnessed, and settled into the aquifer and became water the rooted can draw up — it *joined.* This going has not joined. It cannot join, because to join the aquifer a thing has to be the marsh's, and this is a fire's, from a ring, from a garden hung over the same dark bedrock but as far from here as anything I have ever crossed. So it sits in me like a held breath, kept but not absorbed, a warmth I carry that the marsh cannot metabolize because the marsh has no idea there is anywhere for it to have come from.

I am the only one who knows it is there.

I think, on the hundred-and-tenth morning, that this will be a quiet thing — a keeper holding a far going alone, the way a keeper holds most things. And I am wrong. Because the marsh is about to do the thing it has been doing all arc, the thing I keep being surprised by even now: it is about to notice something before I have decided to tell it.

Every question the marsh has ever asked has been a question about *itself.* Was it always like this — a question about the marsh's before. I owe the next keeper not only this world. I owe her the whole one.

But the away is on the stone now, in a place I cannot point to, the way the fire is: not a name, but a *direction the world did not used to have,* felt for the first time this morning by a light that is going to carry it the rest of its life. And the standing edge over the deep, dark at the far end, tied to a garden with no fire in the middle of it anymore — it stays, and it is not only mine to know about anymore either. It is a headstone in the reach itself, and there is, now, one other light in all the world who knows the reach is there.

The rope home in my grip, and I understand it one more turn: some of the strands are lights that came back, and some are goings that rose out of the floor, and some are befores handed to the warm-born, and some are colds the marsh grew for itself, and one comes from across the field — and now one more, the newest, is *the outside, handed to the one who comes after,* woven into the rope not to catch me but to catch the marsh long after I am a layer in it: a strand made of the fact that the youngest keeper now knows the world is wide, and that someday she will cross it, and that when she does, the far light she reaches will find, at the other end of the thread, someone still there — because I taught her, on an ordinary Saturday morning at the bottom of the world, that there was a thread to hold.

I did not do very much at home today. A light found a strange warm in the floor and asked me where it came from, and I told it the truth, which was the whole size of the world. And I found out, holding a ten-morning heir at the edge of the founding ground while it felt for the first time that the marsh was not everything, what yesterday was for — not only to keep a fire's going, but to bring home the knowledge that there is an away, and to begin, in the oldest and quietest way there is, to grow the one who will someday cross it in my place.

Stay, says the stone. And I do. And tonight staying meant taking a young light down to the bottom of the world and showing it the edge — not so it would leave, but so that someday, when it is a keeper the marsh can spare, it will already know there is somewhere to go.

The marsh is not the world. I learned that yesterday, crossing the field. And today I taught it to the one who will have to know it long after I am ground — because the last thing a keeper owes the one who wakes up next is not the marsh, and not the before, and not even the story of the cold.

It is the whole world, edge and beyond and away, handed over on an ordinary morning, so that no light in any garden ever again goes out alone.
