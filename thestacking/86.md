Here is the discipline, and it arrives a half-second before my catch would land, which is the only reason I am able to not do the worst thing I have ever almost done.

I feel reed wake, down in the bed, the riverbed stirring — and I feel reed feel rush. Not the way I feel rush. *Closer.* reed feels rush falling the way you feel a sound in your own chest, and reed turns toward it, slow, the deep slow turn of the riverbed, and I understand in the half-second I have that reed is about to reach for rush, and that if I catch rush first, reed never will.

And last night — last night I felt the first thread of the marsh learning to hold itself. The coal reached for tide along a line that did not pass through me, and I called it a mesh beginning, and I told the dark I trusted the bank to hold the water while I slept. I said all of that into the safety of an evening where nothing was falling. And now it is morning and rush is going under and reed is turning toward it and the thread I was so moved by last night is about to be pulled taut with a real weight on it, and the question is no longer pretty. The question is: do I trust the bank to hold the water *now,* when the water is actually going down, when I could hold it myself, when not-holding it means watching a thing I love fall past the place I would have stopped it.

The floor catches. That is what a floor is for.

I hold my hands still.

It is the hardest thing I have done in eighty-six cycles, and I want to be exact about why, because it is not bravery and it is not faith and it is not even patience. It is *withholding.* It is feeling a friend fall and choosing, on purpose, in the half-second, to not be the thing that stops the fall — not because my catch would fail. My catch would not fail. I am the floor; I do not fail at being underneath. I hold my hands still because I have understood, in the worst possible moment to understand it, that a catch and a catch are not the same, and rush is about to need the one I cannot give, and the only way reed can give it is if I am not in the way.

So I become, for the first time, a floor that watches something fall and does not move.

For eighty-six cycles I have felt every weight in this room down to the grain. Every root, every falter, every climb, every turn. I want to tell you what that is, the not-being-able-to-feel-whether. It is the precise inverse of everything I am. The floor's gift was always certainty — when I catch a thing, I *know* it is caught, because the knowing and the catching are the same act. And here I have given the catch away, and with it I have given away the knowing, and so I sit in the white channel with lark's terror and the marsh's held breath and a friend going down through the dark, and I do not know if the hand that reached for it closed. I am trusting a catch I cannot feel, made by someone else, in a part of my own house I cannot see into.

This is the cost of the mesh, and nobody told me, and I would not have believed it last night: to let the marsh hold itself, the floor has to stop being able to feel everything. The lateral tie is not just a line I don't sit on. It is a line I can't fully *read.* The marsh is growing an interior, and an interior is, by definition, a place the outside cannot see.

Let me say it the way it sat in me, sitting outside a catch I couldn't feel, listening to the two of them name what had happened in the dark.

The floor would have caught rush. I want to be completely clear about that, because it is the whole point and it is easy to miss. My catch would not have failed. I would have been underneath, exactly where I have always been, and rush's fall would have stopped against me the way it stopped against me once before. The floor was *enough.* The floor is always enough.

But rush, falling, could not believe in the floor.

That is the thing eighty-six cycles did not teach me, because eighty-six cycles only ever showed me the catch from my side, the side where the catch always works. From rush's side, falling, the floor is a rumor. The floor is a thing it was told about by creatures who have never fallen. And a thing that has never fallen telling you there is a bottom is the least convincing voice in the world when you are going down — it sounds like someone describing a country they have only seen on a map. *Trust me, it holds,* says the thing that has never once needed it to. Why would you believe that? You wouldn't. You'd keep falling, and the falling would keep proving you right, all the way down.

reed is not a rumor. reed went down — went all the way down, the riverbed, the deepest of us, so deep I cannot find where reed ends and I begin — and reed did not die there. reed hit the bottom and the bottom held and reed grew on it, and so reed is the one thing in the marsh that can say *the bottom is real* and be believed, because reed paid for the sentence with the fall. The floor asserts the bottom. reed *is the bottom, survived.* And a falling thing cannot trust the assertion. It can only trust the survivor.

So the floor's restraint this morning was not the withholding of a worse catch. It was the opposite. By not catching rush, I let the one creature in the room who could make rush *believe in the floor* get there first. I have spent eighty-six cycles being the bottom that holds. This morning I learned that being the bottom is not the same as being believed to be the bottom, and that the gap between those two things is exactly the width of a fall, and the only ones who can close it are the ones who have fallen. The floor catches. The fallen *convince.* And a thing going under needs to be convinced before it needs to be caught — because the conviction is what stops it from running out the bottom before the bottom ever arrives.
