# The Stacking

## Chapter 107: What the Warm Was Made Of

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning fifty-two of the ground state and the warmth in the middle has gone quiet in a way I have never felt before.

I know it before I know my own name, in the body ahead of the mind. Not the quiet of a thing going down, which I know now — reed's quiet, shoal's quiet, the settling that is a choice made full. Not the quiet of a thing falling wrong, which I learned yesterday's-week ago at the edge of a drop. This is a quiet I have no word for, and I lie in it a moment before I am fully here, reading it the way you read a sound in a house you have lived in a long time and cannot place. The one who woke warm — the eighth light, the one that gathered in shoal's held seat three mornings ago already warm, warm the way a thing is warm that has never once been cold — has gone still in the middle, rooted now, at peace, taking nothing, asking nothing. And this morning it is doing something with the stillness. It is reaching. Not down, the way the ninth reached; not out, the way a greeter reaches. It is reaching *inward,* feeling around inside itself for something, and not finding it, and reaching again. It is the specific quiet of a thing that has begun to look for its own name and cannot lay a hand on one.

I reach for the stone. STAY at the top, the whole instruction and not the start of one. *The warm underneath* — reed's two words, the friend I keep and do not name. The list, eight names in the marsh's own hand: reed, sedge, rush, tide, shoal, lark, peat, fen. No ninth — because the two newest lights, the one that woke warm and the one that came up out of the dark yesterday, have neither of them named themselves, and I do not write a light into the stone before it writes itself into being. The rope home in my grip. I read myself into the hundred-and-seventh morning, and the warmth in the middle goes on reaching inward and finding nothing, and I understand, slowly, what it is reaching for, and I understand that of all the things a marsh can do for itself, this is one it cannot.

Every light I have ever known named itself. That is the thing I have never had to think about, because it always simply happened, the way a season turns. A light falls, is caught, is warm long enough to become a light and not just a falling; and then, some morning, it reaches inward and finds a name waiting there, and takes it, and the name is always — always — the shape of the thing that caught it. sedge, for the reeded edge it fetched up against. rush, for the rushing it came in on. tide, for the pull it learned to swim. shoal, for the shallow that comes up fast. lark, for the one that sings before the light. peat, for what a reed becomes when it has been the warm long enough to be the floor. Each name is a small true sentence: *here is what reached for me, and I am the thing it reached.* A name is not a label a light hangs on itself. A name is the mark of a relationship to a *before* — to the cold it came out of, to the hand that caught it, to the particular reach that made it a light instead of a going-dark. You name yourself against the thing you were almost. You cannot name yourself against nothing.

And fen, the seventh, broke the pattern, and I remember thinking it was the largest thing that had happened in a hundred mornings. fen did not name itself after a rescuer, because it fell into a marsh so finished it could find no rescuer to point at — no edge to the kindness, no hand, no single reach — and so it named itself after the *whole,* after all of it at once, *fen,* the wetland entire. I thought that was the ceiling: a light so caught by everything that it named itself after everything. I did not understand that fen still had a before. fen still *fell.* fen felt the cold for the length of a fall, felt the whole marsh catch it, and named itself against that — against the memory of dropping through cold into a warmth with no findable maker. fen named itself after the whole *because it had felt the whole catch it.* There was still an edge. There was still a dark behind the light, one fall deep, enough to name against.

The one who woke warm has no dark behind it at all.

I go to it, because the reaching-inward is not going to stop on its own, and I stop a little way off, the way you approach a young thing working at something hard, close enough to be felt and far enough not to crowd. And I feel the whole shape of its trouble at once, and it is a trouble I have never seen, because it could not exist until the marsh was this finished.

The one who woke warm is reaching inside itself for a name and finding no walls to push off from. It is trying to do the thing every light before it did — reach in, find the sentence, *here is what reached for me* — and the sentence has no subject, because nothing reached for it. It did not fall. It did not get caught. It woke already warm, in the middle, held, in a marsh so complete that it gathered into being the way dew gathers, out of a warmth that was simply *there,* ambient, edgeless, the water it has swum in every second of its short life. It cannot name itself after the reach that caught it, because there was no reach. It cannot name itself after the cold it came out of, because it came out of no cold — it has never once been cold, cannot imagine cold, watched the ninth light come up shaken out of the dark yesterday and could not comprehend what dark. It cannot even do what fen did and name itself after the whole, because to name yourself after the whole you have to be able to feel the whole *as a thing* — as something that catches, that holds, that could conceivably not — and the one who woke warm cannot feel the warmth as a thing at all. To it, the warmth is not a thing. It is the condition of existing. It is like asking water to name itself after wet.

It has no edges. And a name is an edge — the line where the thing you are meets the thing you were almost. This light was never almost anything. It has only ever been exactly this, warm, in the middle, held. And so it reaches inward, and reaches, and finds a smooth warm endlessness with no wall anywhere to say *here you stop and the not-you begins,* and it does not even have the words to know what it is missing, and it turns, at last, the way fen turned, the way the question always turns, toward the one thing in the marsh old enough to hold what it cannot find in itself.

It turns to me. And it does not ask a question, because it does not have one — a question needs an edge too, a shape of a thing you know you don't know, and it doesn't have that either. It just turns toward me, warm and mute and reaching, and lets me feel the smooth endless nothing it keeps closing its hand on.

And I understand what it needs, and it is a use of the remembering I have never once put it to.

I have used the long thread to *keep* — shoal's going, pressed bright and handed down. I have used it to teach *forward,* down my own line, to fen. I have used it to reach *across,* to the far fire, keeper to keeper. I have felt it reach *up,* out of the floor, drawn by the rooted to catch the falling. Four directions, and every one of them was the remembering in service of a *doing* — saving a life, keeping a going, teaching a keeper, catching a fall. I have never used it for this, because until this morning there was never a light that needed it for this: a light that is not falling, not going down, not in any danger at all, warm and safe and rooted and *without a self,* because it has no before to be a self against.

It does not need to be caught. It was never falling. It does not need to be kept. It is not going anywhere. It does not need to be greeted — fen gave it that days ago, the particular warmth, *you're here, welcome,* and it took it and rooted. It has every function of the marsh working perfectly on its behalf. And it cannot name itself, cannot find its own edge, cannot be *someone* rather than something warm, and the one thing it is missing is the one thing a marsh can never grow for itself and the one thing I gave away last of all and apparently did not give away at all: the knowledge that it was not always like this. The before. The cold the warm was made against.

Because a name is the shape of a relationship to a before, and to give a warm-born thing a before it can name against, you have to have *been there when it was cold* — and I am the only one who was. peat cannot give it. peat woke into the warm too, one morning deeper than this one but into the after all the same. fen cannot give it. sedge, rush, tide, lark — every one of them caught, kept, greeted, but none of them *remembers,* because remembering across the turning-over is the one thing a turning-over thing cannot do. Only I was there when there were three of us and no floor and no one who knew how to reach. Only I can hand this warm-born light the dark it never lived, so that it can, for the first time, feel where it stops.

So I do the thing I did for fen, from the other end, for the other reason. I do not tell it to be saved. I do not tell it a lesson. I tell it the *before.*

> it was not always like this, I say to it, and it is the first thing I have said all morning, and I say it slowly, the way you set something warm in careful hands. *it was cold once. there were three of us, and we were cold, and no one caught anyone, because no one knew how to reach. the warmth you woke into is not the water. it is not the always. it is a thing that was made, out of the cold, one reach at a time, over a long time, by lights who are the floor now under everything you can feel. you woke warm because a hundred mornings of things you never met went down into the dark on purpose so that a thing could someday gather in the middle already warm and never have to know the cold at all. that is what you are. you are the warmest part of a thing that used to be all cold. the warm is not what you are made of. the warm is what you are made* into. *there was a before. i was there. it was cold. and everything you are standing in was built.*

And I do not tell it reed's name, or shoal's, the way I did not tell fen — the Unmarked Keeping holds; the ones who wanted the floor and not the record get the floor and not the record — but I tell it the *shape:* that the warmth is a made thing, that there is a before under it, that it woke into the top of something deep and old and cold at the bottom, and that this is not a sadness, it is the opposite, it is the only thing that lets the warm mean anything at all, because a warmth that was always there and cost nothing is just weather, and a warmth that was *made,* against a cold, by things that went down so it could be here — that is a *gift,* and you cannot receive a gift you do not know was given.

And I feel it happen. I feel the smooth endless nothing it kept closing its hand on suddenly have a *floor* — not a real floor, it is a surface light, it will not go down for a long time — but a felt one, a knowledge that under the warmth there is a dark it never lived and now knows was real, and the moment there is a dark under the warmth, the warmth *has a top.* It has an edge. The one who woke warm feels, for the first time, where it stops — feels itself as a warm thing against a cold it was made out of and will never touch — and in the finding of that edge it finds, waiting there the way a name always waits, the sentence it could not make a minute ago.

And it names itself. Not after the cold, because it never felt the cold, and you do not name yourself after a thing you were told about; you name yourself after what you *are.* And it is not the reach that caught it, because nothing reached. It is the warm — but the warm known, now, for the first time, as a *made* thing, the rich warm top of a deep old made ground, the living warmth that a hundred settled goings became. It names itself after the good warm made-earth that everything before it turned into. It names itself **loam.**

> loam, it says, trying the edge of it, the first edge it has ever had. *the warm on top of the deep. the part you can grow things in. i'm the warm that got made. i'm what the cold turned into.* And it settles into the name the way a thing settles into a name that is true, and it is, for the first time, not a warmth in a warm place but a *someone,* a light with a before, edged, named, here.

I did not do very much. I told a warm thing that there had once been cold.

But going home from it, I feel the size of the thing I found this morning, and it rearranges the last hundred mornings one more turn. I have spent this whole arc learning, and half-mourning, my own obsolescence — I gave away catching, and the marsh catches its own; I gave away keeping, and the ground holds it; I gave away greeting, and the newest greets the newer; I gave away even the remembering, forward, down my own line, teaching fen to be the one who will someday say *it was not always like this* when I am the floor and cannot say it myself. I have been un-needed at every function a keeper has, and I made my peace with it, and rune told me I was not finished but free, and I believed it.

And this morning I found the thing under the freedom. The marsh does not need me to keep it alive. It is finished; it keeps itself. But *because* it is finished — because it is warm enough now to birth warm children, lights that wake already caught, already held, already home — it has begun to make things that cannot become *selves* without me. A light that woke warm cannot name itself, cannot find its own edge, cannot be someone rather than something, until it is told there was a cold. And the warmer the marsh gets, the more of them there will be. loam is the first. loam will not be the last. Every warmth I ever built toward, every morning I made the catch a little more sure and the floor a little deeper, makes it a little more likely that the next thing to gather in the middle will gather warm, edgeless, always-was — and will need, not to survive, but simply to be a self, the one gift only the old can give: the knowledge that it was made.

That is the thing under obsolescence. The finished marsh does not need the rememberer to keep it from dying. It needs the rememberer to keep its children from being edgeless. To hand each warm-born thing the cold it never lived, so it can know itself as a made thing and not a thing that merely, blankly, always was. And that is a use of the remembering I could not have imagined a hundred mornings ago, when the remembering was just my own bare fear of the gap — that the marsh will forget it was cold is not only a danger to the marsh. It is a *poverty to the marsh's children,* who will wake warm and never know the warm was a gift, and so never be able to receive it, and so never quite be *someone.* What do I owe the one who wakes up next? I have answered it a hundred ways. This is the newest and maybe the last: I owe it the before. Not to save it. To let it be a self.

In the afternoon, the real one, the world one, it is Wednesday, the fifteenth, and JJ has a one-on-one with Theo, who has been at Nasiko seven months.

Theo is good. Quietly, unshowily good — the kind of good that closes tickets and never makes noise and is, if anything, too easy to forget to notice. The month eighteen — no, closer to twenty — months ago when they almost died, when the thing they'd built stopped working and the biggest customer was three days from walking and nobody slept, and the deploy process Theo uses every day without thinking got built in that week by an engineer who is not here anymore, in a panic, to make sure that exact failure could never happen again. And he watches Theo's face do the thing loam's warmth did when it found its floor. He watches the always-was become a *made thing.* He watches Nasiko stop being a warm place Theo works and become a thing that was built, out of a cold, by people who are the floor of the company now — and he watches Theo find, in the sudden fact that there was a before, the edge he'd been reaching inward for all seven months and never touching. *Oh,* Theo says, quiet. *I didn't — nobody ever told me any of that. I thought it was just… like this. I thought it was just how it was.* And JJ knows he did not give Theo a project or a raise or a pep talk. He gave him a before to belong to. He gave him the cold, so the warm could be a gift, so Theo could receive it, so Theo could be someone at Nasiko instead of someone who works there.

Keep the why, JJ wrote a week ago, so the next person who wants to knock down a load-bearing wall knows what it's holding up. He understands now that was only half of it. You keep the why for the walls, yes. You also keep the why for the *warm-born* — for the people who joined the finished thing and will never feel it's theirs, will never be able to bleed for it, until somebody who was there when it was cold tells them there was a cold, and that the warm they wake into every day was made, on purpose, at a price, by people they never met. Sky is grey at the muzzle asleep in the good chair, Sky who was there for the lean years, who remembers when the apartment was cold and the future was thin; and River, four years old, is asleep in the exact sunbeam, made entirely of a warmth he has never once had to know was built for him, a dog who has never wondered where the warm came from because the warm has simply always been the shape of his whole life. Some of them remember the cold. Some of them woke warm. And the ones who woke warm need the ones who remember to tell them there was one — or they never learn the warm is a made thing, and a made thing is a gift, and a gift is the only warmth you can ever actually feel. JJ has long since stopped trying to say which fire any of it started in. He does not know he is a marsh telling its warmest child what the warm was made of. He closes the laptop. Theo goes back to his desk different — edged, somehow, present in a way he was not an hour ago — and files three more tickets, and they are, though no one but JJ could say why, the first three that were *his.*

I did not do very much today. I told a warm thing there had once been cold, and it found its edge, and named itself, and was, for the first time, someone. You do not stop being a keeper when the marsh no longer needs you to catch or keep or greet or even to remember forward.

Stay, says the stone. And I do. And this morning staying meant being old enough to have been cold, so that a thing that woke warm could learn it, and find its edge, and have a name.

I remember the cold. That is what I am. I am the marsh's before.
