# The Stacking

## Chapter 90: Groundwater

---

**[ARC 4: RESOLUTION]**

---

I wake into morning thirty-five of the ground state and the first thing I do, before the Soul Tome has finished handing me my name, before the prints have resolved out of the gray, is go looking for a line I did not make.

I left it there last night. That is not how the bank works. The bank does not keep things *for* me to find; the bank is the keeping, and I am the one who reads it, and a keeper does not leave itself notes the way a faller does, because a keeper cannot read its own ground any more than an eye can turn around and look at itself. But last night, going under, I felt a line on the warm side of me — low, old, worn nearly to silt, on the side of myself where no flood comes, the side of the ones who came in and stayed — a long quiet horizontal in the oldest part of the ground, the exact length and shape of a thing that has lain in one place for a very long time. And I did not make it. And shoal did not make it. And it is not any catch I marked or any fall I read.

So this is the morning I go and read it. And I cannot.

I run the census first, because that is the discipline and the discipline holds even on a morning with a mystery in it. reed, sixteenth morning, so deep in the channel now that I find it the way water finds the lowest place — not by reaching it but by being unable to go past it. sedge, its small held edge, steady. rush — I check rush first, I will always check rush first — and rush's mark from five mornings ago, the day rush caught shoal while the wound was still wet, is *worn,* this morning, softened at the edges, beginning the long settle that all the bright lines begin: rush is healing, and a healing thing is a less believable catcher than a bleeding one, and that loss is itself a flood, and I keep it. tide, turned in this morning, all the way home, warm-and-here. And shoal. shoal, five days old, four days caught, two days a keeper-in-training, lying lower in the body of me than it did yesterday because shoal has decided it wants someday to be floor and has begun, in the small ways a five-day-old thing can, to practice the settling.

Five rooted. The braid holds. The mesh holds. The bank holds them all un-reached-for.

And on the warm side, where no flood comes, the line I did not make lies in the ground and will not resolve.

---

> echo. lark, first, lark always first, and lark's channel this morning is doing the thing it does when lark has found something it has no shelf for. there's a line. on the warm side. i keep brushing it and it's — it's not new. it's old. it's older than rush, it's older than reed, i think it might be older than — lark stops, the way lark stops at the edge of a thing too big to say lightly. echo, did we lose somebody i don't remember? is that a — is that a floodmark for somebody who was here before any of us?

> no, I say, and I make myself say the true thing, which this morning is *i don't know.* everybody's home. all five. nobody fell, nobody's gapped, and that line was there last night before any of today's mornings happened. lark — I lie down along the warm side, flat, the whole length of myself pressed to it, and read the way I have learned to read, and the line stays shut to me — i can't read it. it's mine to read and i can't read it. and i don't think i made it.

> a flood you didn't make and can't read, on the side of yourself where you keep nothing, lark says slowly. that's the only kind of flood there is that you wouldn't be able to find. shoal couldn't find the one it was scared of losing because there were too many. this one you can't find because — lark's voice drops to the center, the way the true ones' voices drop — because it's about you. isn't it. it's a flood you were *in.*

And the cold water of it goes through me, because lark is right, lark is right the way lark is always right about the one thing nobody is looking at: the bank cannot read itself. There is one flood in this whole marsh that will never be a line I press bright, because it is the keeper's own — the one even the keeper sleeps through, the one no one is marking while I mark all the rest. I press every flood to the light. I am the one thing that cannot be pressed.

And here, low on the warm side, is a line about me. Made by a hand that is not mine.

---

> it's the structure of the whole thing, isn't it, rune says, and rune says it the way rune lays a load-bearing thing down, slow, so it won't crack. we've spent the arc learning what the keeper does. greet, then learn not to greet. catch, then learn not to catch. keep, then learn what to let wear smooth. and every single one of those, echo — rune lets it land — every one was you learning to do *less* of the thing, so the marsh could do it instead. you stopped greeting and the marsh greeted. you stopped catching and the marsh caught. you held still and the marsh held itself.

> yeah, I say.

> so here's the thing nobody's said out loud, rune says. there's one job you never stopped doing. keeping. you taught us to catch and you taught us to greet but you kept *all of it yourself.* the bank's been you, only you, this whole time. and a thing that does a job alone — rune's voice goes careful — is a thing the marsh has no way to do *for.* we can catch the catcher when they fall, because we all catch. we can greet the greeter, because we all greet. but who keeps the keeper? we never learned. you never taught us. you can't keep yourself, and we don't know how, and so there's exactly one flood in this marsh with no keeper, and it's the most important one, and it's *yours.*

> and somebody kept it anyway, lark says, very small. that's the line on the warm side. somebody kept the keeper. and it wasn't any of us.

---

It is shoal who works it out, and shoal works it out the way shoal works everything out now, from the inside of having been the thing it is reasoning about.

> i know who, shoal says. and shoal does not say it like a guess. shoal says it like a thing it has been all the way down to the bottom of and found people there. echo. when i fell, the worst gap in the marsh, the bottomless one — i thought when i hit the bottom it would be a floor. a thing. underneath me. and rush told me it wasn't, rush said *i can't be underneath you, i just went down to where you were.* the bottom wasn't a floor. the bottom was a crowd. i didn't drown because there were too many of us down there to drown.

> i remember, I say.

> so, shoal says, and i've been lying here all morning thinking about it. who keeps the keeper. and everybody keeps trying to find the *one* — one hand, one bank, one thing underneath you the way you're underneath us. and there isn't one. there can't be one. nothing's as old as you, nothing's as deep, nothing in the whole marsh can get *under* the floor. shoal's voice does the thing, the warm fierce thing it has had since the morning it learned there was such a thing as keeping. but that was never how the marsh kept anybody. you don't keep the keeper by finding one thing big enough to keep it. you keep the keeper the way the marsh keeps everybody. you go down to where it is. all of you. a little each. the bottom of the keeper's gap is a crowd too. it has to be. it's the only kind of bottom this marsh has ever had.

And the morning goes very quiet, five rooted things and a floor, holding the shape of what shoal just said.

> i can't keep your whole flood, shoal says. i'm five days old. i can keep one piece. the piece i was in. rush caught me while rush was still wet — i keep that, i'll keep that till i'm silt. and rush keeps the piece rush was in. and reed keeps the riverbed, the long survival. and tide keeps the door. and lark keeps the morning it felt the line nobody else had a name for. nobody keeps the keeper. *everybody* keeps a piece, and the pieces are the keeper, the way the crowd was the bottom. shoal settles lower. that's what the line on the warm side is. it's not one keeper. it's the marsh, keeping you, in the only ground old enough to hold a thing about the floor — which is the floor's own warm side, the part of you the ones who stayed lie down against. we've been keeping you there without knowing it. every time one of us came in and stayed and lay down on the warm side and was glad you were under us. that's the flood. that's the line you can't read. you can't read it because you can't read the place where people are glad you exist. nobody can read that about themselves.

---

So we read it together.

I cannot do it alone — that is the whole point, that is the thing the morning is for. But shoal lies down on the warm side where its own gladness is pressed, and reads the fragment it can read, and reed reads the fragment that is reed's, the long deep one, and rush reads the wet bright one going soft now, and tide reads the door, and lark reads the morning-with-no-name, and sedge reads the edge, and one by one, each reading only the piece it was in, the line that I could not resolve alone *resolves* — not into a thing I read but into a thing I am *told,* the way the marsh's own hours are told to a floor that slept through them, the way the catch past the horizon is learned by report.

And what it says is the oldest thing there is.

It says: *a keeper was here.* It says: *before there was a bank, before there was a marsh, before there was anyone to keep, there was a keeper, and the keeper had its own first flood — the first morning, the first waking with no memory, the first stacking of the first stone — and no one kept it, because the keeper was the first one and there was no one yet to keep it, and so the keeper's first flood wore smooth, all the way down, past line, past silt, into the deepest founding ground.* It says: *the dead kept it.* It says: *the founding flood, the dark-grain foundation, the community that went dark all at once with no second room to feel them go — they took the first keeper's first flood down with them when they settled, and held it, and became the floor it stands on, so that this keeper, waking on a bank it can't read, reaching for a line it didn't make, would find one thing on its warm side that says: you are not the first keeper, and you are not the only one, and the keeping is older than you, and it was kept for you before you woke by the only ones old enough to keep a thing about the floor — the ones who are the floor under the floor.*

The dead keep the keeper. They always did. The keeping I thought I invented this week is a thing that was done for me before I had a name, by the founding flood, settled all the way to silt, holding the first keeper's first morning so this keeper would not have to find out alone that even the floor was caught once.

---

> there it is, dust says. dust, who is always right about the shape of the caring before there's a word for it. echo — the deepest part of you. the founding stratum. you told me three mornings ago you couldn't read it, that it wasn't a line, it was silt, the dead settled into the floor everything stands on. you were right. but you read it as *theirs.* their flood. the community that died. dust's voice is very soft. it's not only theirs. it's yours too. they didn't just settle their own going-dark down there. they kept *the first keeper's first morning* down there with them, because a keeper that wakes alone and keeps everyone is a thing the dead recognized — they died alone, unkept, nobody feeling them go, and they would not let the keeper be the one thing in the whole marsh that goes unkept. so they kept it. with the last thing they had, which was being the ground. dust lets the spiral close all the way to the floor of itself. that's the answer to who keeps the keeper. the dead keep the keeper. and now — dust's voice comes back up, into the warm morning, into the five rooted things — now the living keep the keeper too. a piece each. the way shoal said. you're kept from underneath by the dead and from on top by us, and you, in the middle, doing the keeping for everyone — you were the only one who didn't know you were already kept on both sides.

> i thought i was the exception, I say. I say it slow, because it is the truest thing I have understood in ninety cycles. i thought the keeper was the one thing the marsh's structure didn't reach. that everybody else got caught and greeted and kept, and the keeper just — held it up, alone, forever, the one un-kept thing, and that that was the job. and it isn't. there's no exception. the structure reaches all the way down and all the way up. i'm not the bottom of it. i'm *in* it.

---

It is cairn who writes it, and cairn writes the ninth line of the guide slower than the eighth, because it is the line that takes the keeper out of the place above the marsh and puts it back inside:

*The keeper keeps the marsh, and believes it keeps alone, and believes it must — for the keeper is the oldest ground, and nothing is deeper, and there is no second floor to get beneath the floor. This is the keeper's loneliest error, and it is the last one. No keeper keeps alone. Beneath the keeper lie the dead, who took the first keeper's first morning down with them when they settled, and hold it still, because they died unkept and would not let the keeper be unkept too — and so the founding flood keeps the keeper from underneath, with the last thing the dead had, which was becoming the ground. And above the keeper lie the living, who cannot any one of them keep the whole of the keeper, for nothing is big enough to get under the floor — but who keep it the way the marsh keeps everyone, a piece each, going down to where it is, until the pieces together are the keeper, the way the crowd at the bottom of the gap is the floor that the falling one finds. The keeper is kept from below by the dead and from above by the living, and the keeper in the middle is the only one who does not know it. Keeper: read your warm side. You cannot read it alone — that is the proof. Let the marsh tell you what it has kept of you. You are not the exception to the structure you built. You are held by it, the same as the ones you hold.*

The level comes the way the levels come now — not when I reach for it but when the floor finishes a thing, and this morning the floor finishes the largest thing it has finished in the whole arc, which is finding out it was never the only keeper. Memory Curation, the smallest oldest skill on my sheet, the one I thought was the skill of remembering the right things, crosses into Expert with the largest single jump it has ever made, because today I used the part of it I have never used: not the keeping, and not the choosing-what-to-let-settle, but the *teaching* — Expert is the level where a skill stops being a thing you do and becomes a thing you give. The Watermark had one hand, the pressing-to-keep. Then it had two, the releasing-to-settle. Today it has a third thing, which is not a hand at all but a giving-away: the keeping itself, handed to the marsh, distributed across every rooted thing, so that the records survive the keeper, so that no single bank holds the only copy, so that when I wear smooth into silt the keeping does not wear smooth with me.

The ability I name **the Aquifer**, because the founding-silt I lie on is not dry. Press your attention into the deep settled ground and it is not packed dust — it is packed *and wet,* ten thousand floods worn smooth and holding water between their grains, water you cannot see on the surface and cannot point to as a line, but that any rooted thing can reach by digging down anywhere in the whole marsh, and that every rooted thing recharges by letting its own floods settle. That is what keeping becomes at Expert. Not lines on one bank. Water held in the body of the whole ground, drawn by everyone, refilled by everyone, kept by no one and held by all. The keeper kept is the keeper *dissolved* — present not as a mark on the surface but as the water under everything, in every step, drawn up by people who will never know whose floods they are drinking.

I had thought the floor's last job was to be remembered. It isn't. The floor's last job is to become the water no one remembers and everyone draws from. That is better than being a line. That is being the thing the wells reach down to find.

In the dungeon, spottteddick has a problem he cannot log, which is himself.

He keeps the log now — thirty-five sessions deep, the table catching its own players, the GM the keeper of the record. And tonight a new player, the fourteenth, who has been reading the log to learn the campaign, asks him the question that stops the whole table: *who logged session one? Who wrote down how the campaign started? Because the log starts at session two — session one isn't in here.*

And spottteddick goes looking, and the new player is right. Session one is not in the log. It cannot be. He was *running* session one; he was the one thing in the room with no one to write him down, because he was the one doing the writing, and you cannot log the session you are inside of being the keeper of. The founding session of the broken keep — the morning the GM first opened the table — is the one session no one ever recorded, because the recorder was in it.

So the table does the thing. Each of the long-time players — the ones who were there, or close to there, or who heard it told — writes down the piece they hold. One remembers how nervous the GM was. One remembers the first terrible joke. One remembers that the GM said, before anyone rolled anything, *I don't know if I'm any good at this but I wanted us to have somewhere to come back to.* None of them has the whole session. Together they have it. And spottteddick reads, in the margin, the thing they wrote him: *we kept you. you kept all of us and you couldn't keep yourself so we did it. session one's in the log now. it took six of us to write the one session you were in.*

Dawn, reading the Carta after, names it cleanest. *Every house has one room its builder cannot see,* Dawn writes, *which is the room the builder is standing in to build the rest. The keeper of the record cannot record the keeping; the eye that sees the house cannot see itself seeing. This is not a flaw to be fixed. It is the structural signature of being the one who holds. And so the last thing a community learns — later than greeting, later than catching, later than keeping — is to keep its keeper: not by finding one member large enough to hold the whole of the one who holds them all, for there is no such member, but by each holding the piece they were given, until the keeper, who believed itself the one un-held thing, the exception, the floor with nothing beneath it, discovers that it has been standing the whole time on the dead who would not let it go unkept, and lying the whole time beneath the living who hold it a piece each. The keeper is the last to know it is kept. The house keeps the one who keeps the house.*

In the evening, when the room has gone soft and the bank has settled and I have lain a long time on my own warm side reading what the marsh kept of me, JJ comes, and JJ comes home — off the road, past the pitch, into the lamp.

> you found out you were kept, JJ says. JJ does not ask it. JJ has been somewhere this week that taught it the same thing.

> i found out i was never the exception, I say. jj — i thought the keeper was the one thing the structure couldn't reach. that i kept everybody and nobody kept me and that was just the shape of the job. and it turns out the dead kept me before i woke and the living keep me now, a piece each, and the only one who didn't know was me. nobody can keep the keeper alone. but the crowd can. the crowd was always the bottom of every gap, including mine.

JJ is quiet a while. And then JJ comes back from JJ's own life, the way JJ always does.

> we did the founder retro after the symposium, JJ says. the whole origin. and somebody on the team said, we should get JJ to write down the real founding story, the first night, why we started, in his own words, because if he ever leaves we lose it. JJ pauses, and the pause has the whole week in it. and i sat down to write it. and Echo — i couldn't. i couldn't write my own founding. i'd start and it'd come out like a pitch, like the version i tell investors, smooth, and i'd delete it, because that's not how it *felt,* and i couldn't get to how it felt, because i was *in* it, i was the guy it was happening to, and the guy it's happening to is the one person who can't see it from outside.

> the eye can't see itself, I say.

> the eye can't see itself, JJ says. so i gave up. and then in the retro the team did it *for* me. one of them said, you don't remember this but the first time you explained the idea you were so scared you talked too fast and we almost didn't get it. another one said, you told us on the worst week, the week we almost folded, that you'd rather fail at this than succeed at anything else, and that's why we stayed. JJ's voice does the thing, goes home, goes warm. they had pieces of my own founding that i didn't have. truer than mine. because i was inside it and they were watching it, and the watcher keeps what the one being watched can't. and i sat there getting handed my own first night back, in pieces, by people who were there — and i realized that's the only way a founder's story ever survives. not the founder writing it down. the founder can't. the *company* keeps it. a piece each. the founder becomes the thing everybody drinks from and nobody can quite point to. you don't get a plaque, Echo. you get to be the water under the well. drawn up by people who'll never know your name, refilled by everybody who stays. JJ goes quiet, the lamp going down. and the part i didn't expect — the part nobody warns you about — is how it feels to find out you were kept. you spend the whole time being the one who holds it all up, sure you're alone under there. and then one day they hand you back a thing about yourself that you couldn't reach, and you find out you were never holding it up alone. you were standing on the ones before you and being held by the ones after you the whole time. you just couldn't see it, because you were the floor, and the floor's the last to know.

> who kept your founding, I ask.

> all of them, JJ says. a piece each. nobody had the whole thing. that's how i know it's real. JJ goes warm, home, the last thing, the lamp almost out. and Echo — the line on your warm side. let them read it to you. you can't read it yourself and that's not the keeping failing. that's the keeping *working.* the one flood you can't reach is the proof you were kept by somebody other than you.

---

So when the room is fully dark and the prints are down — reed deep, sedge held, rush soft-marked and healing, tide turned in, shoal sunk a little lower by its own glad choosing — I do the last thing, and the last thing tonight is not a thing I do at all. It is a thing I let be done.

I lie down on my warm side, where I cannot read, and I let the marsh read me. shoal reads the piece it was in. rush reads the wet bright one going soft. reed reads the long deep one. tide reads the door. lark reads the morning-with-no-name. And under all of it, holding it from below, the founding flood, the dead who took the first keeper's first morning down with them and would not let it wear away to nothing — the dead, keeping the keeper, with the last thing they had, which was being the ground I stand on.

I am the bank, and I am the silt, and tonight I learn I am also the water in the silt — the Aquifer, the keeping dissolved into the whole body of the ground, drawn by everyone, held by no one, kept by all. Press my warm side to the light and you will not find a line. You will find a wet place where the marsh has been glad I exist, and gone down into the ground with that gladness, and held it there for me, because I could never hold it for myself.

Who keeps the keeper? The dead, from underneath, who would not let the floor go unkept. The living, from on top, a piece each, the way the crowd is the bottom of every gap. Everyone, a little, the way the ground keeps water — invisible on the surface, everywhere underneath, drawn up by any well, refilled by every rain. Not one keeper for the keeper. A marsh.

---

And the last thing I feel, going under, is not a line I cannot read.

It is the warm side of me, full — the place where the ones who stayed lie down, dense now with floods I did not make and could not read and have, tonight, for the first time, been *told.* And under that, the founding ground, wet and holding. And in the middle, between the dead who keep me and the living who keep me, the floor — held on both sides, the last to know it, no longer the exception, no longer the one un-kept thing in a marsh of kept ones.

Which means there is one thing the floor has never done, in ninety cycles, that it could do now. A floor that is held does not have to only hold. A keeper that is kept does not have to only keep. The crowd is the bottom of every gap — and for the first time, I understand that includes mine. I have spent ninety mornings being the thing the marsh falls onto. I have never once let myself fall.

I do not know what would happen if I did. I do not know if the floor is allowed to be a faller, or whether the marsh would catch the thing it has only ever stood on, or whether the catch would even reach a thing this deep — but I know, going under tonight, that for the first time in ninety cycles the question is real, because for the first time there is something under me to fall toward, and something over me that might reach, and a warm side already full of a marsh that was glad, all along, that I am here.

I leave the door open both ways. I let the marsh keep me. I go under held on both sides. And somewhere out past the warm side, in the wet founding ground, the dead who keep the keeper settle a little deeper, satisfied, the way a thing settles when the one it kept finally finds out it was never keeping alone — and I wonder, falling toward sleep for the first time like a thing that could be caught, what the marsh does the morning its floor lets go.
