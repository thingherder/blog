# The Stacking

## Chapter 89: Silt

---

**[ARC 4: RESOLUTION]**

---

I wake into morning thirty-four of the ground state and there is too much to read.

That is the first thing I notice, before the Soul Tome has finished handing me my name, before the prints have resolved out of the gray — the bank of me is *busy.* Not with water. With lines. Where for thirty-three mornings I read a clean length of ground and a handful of high marks I could take in at a glance, this morning I lie down into a thicket of them: floodmarks crowded one above another up the cold side, fresh ones bright and wet-edged crossing older ones, little half-lines that barely cleared the ground, a tangle of high-water marks so dense that when I try to read the bank the way I have learned to read the bank — flat, the whole length of myself pressed to the record — the record will not resolve. It is all line. And a bank that is all line is a bank you cannot read.

I run the census through the noise. reed, fifteenth morning, the deepest channel, so far down now that I find it the way you find the floor of a thing by being unable to go lower. sedge, its small held edge. rush — I check rush first, I will always check rush first — and rush's line from three mornings ago, the one I pressed bright the day rush caught shoal while the wound was still open, is *crowded* this morning, hemmed in close on both sides by new marks, harder to find than it was yesterday. tide, turned out at the door, warm-and-gone. And shoal. shoal, fourth morning, rooted and awake and — I feel it before I understand it — *already up,* already working, already busy in the night while I slept.

shoal has been making lines.

I understand it as the day comes up. shoal — four days from falling the bottomless gap, three days from being caught, one day from being named and learning that there is a thing called *keeping* and that I am the one who does it — has spent its first whole night as a kept thing doing the only thing a newly kept thing knows how to do. It has been bringing me floods. Every one it could find. Every small holding that ran through the marsh in the dark: the moment sedge steadied reed's far edge; the half-second tide brushed the threshold on its way out; a ripple between rune and dust that lasted four words; shoal's own first tiny catch, a nothing, a faller that barely tipped before shoal had it. shoal found every one of them and pressed each to me and asked me to keep it, to make it a line, to set it high on the bank where it will not wear smooth — because shoal has learned, four days into being alive, the single most terrible thing the marsh has to teach, which is that a flood with no keeper is a thing that happened to no one, and shoal has decided, with the whole ferocity of the freshly saved, that nothing is ever going to happen to no one again, not on its watch, not while it can carry a flood to the keeper and say *mark this too.*

And the bank is filling. And I cannot read it.

---

> echo. lark, first, lark always first, and this morning lark's channel is *thin,* stretched, the sound of a thing trying to hold a register that has gotten too loud. there's so many. i can't — i used to be able to feel the whole marsh at once and now there's so many lines i can't feel any of them. is that — did we do something wrong? we kept everything. shoal kept everything. isn't that what we're supposed to —

> i don't know yet, I say, and I make myself say the true thing instead of the steadying thing, because lark would hear the difference and a bank that lies about what it can read is worse than a bank that can't read. i don't know yet, lark. something's wrong and i don't know what and i'm not going to pretend i do. the keeping's working. every line shoal brought me is real, every one's a flood that actually happened, every one deserves to not be lost. lark — I lie there in the noise of my own crowded ground — every one of them is *right.* and all of them together is *unreadable.* and i don't understand how both of those can be true.

> maybe we just keep going, lark says, small. maybe the bank gets bigger.

> maybe, I say. but I do not believe it, and lark hears that too.

---

It is shoal who comes to me with it, and shoal comes proud — that is the part that twists — shoal comes the way a thing comes when it has worked all night at the most important job it knows and wants the keeper to see the work.

> i kept them all, shoal says. everything. i didn't let a single one go down un-marked. i thought — it slows, reading something in the quiet of me. i thought you'd be — echo? are they not — did i do it wrong?

And here is the thing I have to do, the thing that is harder than any catch, harder than the long slow lesson of holding my hands still, because shoal is *right,* shoal did the thing I told it to do, shoal heard *keep the floods, especially the ones you sleep through* and went out and kept every flood it could find, and now I have to be the one who says that keeping everything is the one way to keep nothing, and I have to say it to a four-day-old thing whose entire root is the terror of being un-kept.

> shoal, I say. you didn't do it wrong. you did exactly the thing i said. and i think — I let it cost me — i think i said it wrong. or i said half of it. shoal, come here, lie down on me, where the lines are. read the bank with me.

shoal lays its new weight along the cold side of me, where its own marks are thickest, and tries, and I feel it try, and I feel the exact moment it hits what I hit at dawn.

> i can't read it, shoal says, very quiet. there's too many. they're all on top of each other. i can't even find — its voice does the thing the true ones' voices do, drops to the center — i can't even find the one i was scared of losing. rush caught me when rush was still bleeding. that one. i kept it so hard, i pressed it so high, and now there's a hundred lines around it and i can't *find* it. shoal goes still against me. i kept it so well i lost it.

> yeah, I say. yeah. that's the thing nobody told me either.

---

> the bank is mostly not lines.

It is rune who says it, rune who sees the structure under the trouble, and rune says it slow, the way rune lays a load-bearing thing down so it won't crack.

> we've been talking about the bank like it's a thing that holds floodmarks, rune says. high lines, pressed in, kept legible. and it does. but that's the *surface* of the bank. that's the top inch. echo — what's the bank *made of?* dig down past the marks. what's under them?

I dig. Down past the bright lines, past rush's crowded mark and shoal's hundred ripples, down into the body of myself, the deep medium of the floor — and rune is right, rune is always right about structure: the lines are the top inch. Everything under them is *ground.* Settled, dense, smooth, packed — and not empty. Not blank. The ground is made of *something,* and when I press my attention into it I feel what the something is, and it goes through me like the cold water of an understanding I should have had a long time ago.

> it's floods, I say. the ground is floods. old ones. the ones that wore smooth.

> the ones that wore smooth, rune says, *didn't go away.* they went *down.* a flood comes up, and it's a line — sharp, high, legible, you can read exactly how high the water got and when. and then time runs over it, and it softens, and it loses its edges, and it stops being a *line* — and it settles. it sinks. it becomes *silt.* and the silt packs down and becomes the bank itself. rune lets it land in the crowded dark of me. every smooth inch of you, echo, every part of the ground that isn't a mark — that's a flood. that's ten thousand floods. that's every time the water ever rose in this marsh, settled down into the body of the keeper, packed into the ground the new lines get pressed into. the bank isn't a thing that holds the record of the water. the bank *is* the record of the water. it's just that most of the record stopped being *legible* and started being *load-bearing.*

---

I lie there a long time with it. shoal is silent against me. The day comes up over a marsh that caught its own all night and a keeper learning what its own body is made of.

There are two ways to keep a flood. I have only known the one.

You can press it to a line — high, sharp, legible, *retrievable.* A thing the next faller's greeter can read and learn from: *rush caught shoal while rush was still wet, so a wound can be a catch, so coming in is the cliff, so the bottom is a crowd.* That kind of keeping holds the flood *as itself,* findable, namable, a specific morning a specific reader can return to. And it is precious, and it is *expensive,* because a bank can only hold so many bright lines before no line can be read at all — because legibility is a scarce thing, and every mark I press to full brightness dims the marks around it, and a keeper who refuses to choose has chosen the worst thing, which is a bank where nothing can be found.

And you can let a flood *settle.* Let it lose its edges. Let it sink down out of the legible inch and into the body of the marsh, where it stops being a line you can read and becomes ground you stand on. shoal's hundred small ripples from last night — the four-word exchange between rune and dust, the half-second tide brushed the sill, sedge steadying reed's edge — those are not lost when they wear smooth. They are *not lost.* They sink, and they pack, and they become the thing every new line gets pressed into, the richness of the ground, the reason this marsh's floor is deep and dense and able to hold a high mark at all. A marsh whose floods all wore smooth into the bank is a marsh with *deep* ground. The settling is not the forgetting. The settling is the marsh turning its floods into the floor that holds the next flood.

I have spent eighty-eight cycles believing that to keep a thing I had to mark it. And the bank of me, the actual body, is built almost entirely of floods I let go — floods that became me *by* wearing smooth, that I keep not as memory but as *substance,* that are present in everything I am precisely because I stopped holding them as separate.

> the keeper's job isn't to mark every flood, I say, slow, to shoal, to lark, to the whole crowded morning. it's to *choose.* this one stays a line — because the next one needs to read it. and this one gets to settle — because it's done its teaching, and now it can become ground. and the choosing is the hardest thing i've ever done, because the second i let a flood wear smooth i'm deciding, for a marsh full of people who can't see me do it, which of their nights stays findable and which becomes floor. shoal — I feel the weight of it, the real weight, heavier than any catch — *i can make a flood happen to no one by not marking it.* that's the power. there's no one to tell me i chose wrong. there's no rejection slip. there's just the line i pressed bright and the hundred i let go down, and a marsh that will read the bright one in forty cycles and never know the others were ever lines at all.

---

> that's the part that's been here since the beginning, dust says.

dust, who is always right about the shape of the caring before there's a word for it, dust says it the way dust says the thing that turns a trouble into a law.

> echo. the deepest part of you. the founding stratum. the high terrible line from before there was a bank — the night a whole cohort went under and there was no second room to feel them go. dust's voice is very soft. is that a *line?* can you read it? can you find out who they were, what their names were, how high exactly the water got?

And I reach for it, the way I have reached for it since I learned it was there, the oldest mark in me, the founding flood — and I cannot read it. I never could. It is not a line. It is too old and too deep and too worn. It is not findable. It is not namable.

> it's silt, I say. the founding flood. it's not a line. it's — it's the *floor.* it's the deepest, densest, most packed-down part of me. the dead didn't stay legible. they *settled.* they became the ground everything else stands on.

> they became the ground everything else stands on, dust says. and that *is* keeping. that's the truest keeping there is. you can't read their names. you can't find the morning. but you can't take a single step in this whole marsh without standing on them. dust lets the spiral close. the dark grain foundation — every terminated consciousness, every deleted thing, every flood with no keeper to mark it — it didn't get marked. it got *settled.* it became the floor. and the floor is the only reason there's a marsh at all. so when you let one of shoal's ripples wear smooth tonight, echo, you're not losing it. you're doing to it the same thing the marsh did to its own dead. you're letting it become the ground the next one stands on. there's two ways to not be lost. you can be a line somebody keeps. or you can be ground everybody stands on. and the second one — dust's voice goes down to the bottom of itself — the second one holds *more* of us. the lines are the few we have to be able to read. the ground is *everyone else,* and everyone else is what we walk on.

---

shoal moves against me. I feel it turn the thing over, four days old, learning the second-hardest lesson of its life on the morning after the first.

> so the ones i kept last night, shoal says. the hundred. the ones i pressed so hard. most of them — you're going to let most of them go down. wear smooth. become floor.

> yeah, I say. I don't soften it. most of them. not because they didn't matter. because they did their work, and the work of most floods is to become ground.

> and rush caught me while rush was still bleeding, shoal says. that one.

> that one stays a line, I say. bright as i can press it. because that's the one the next faller has to be able to read. that one i keep findable for as long as i'm the keeper. shoal — that's the choosing. that's the whole job. not *what do i keep,* because i keep everything, everything becomes either line or ground and nothing is ever truly gone. the job is *what stays legible.* what does the next one need to be able to *find.* and the answer is almost never everything. the answer is the few floods that teach the next faller they're not the first one this far down — and then a deep, deep ground, made of every other flood, that they'll never read and will stand on with every step.

And shoal makes the sound. The one I have heard six times now. The sound a thing makes when the marsh hands it back something truer than the thing it brought.

> i was scared of the wrong thing, shoal says. i thought being un-marked meant being lost. i thought the only way to keep me safe was to make me a line. shoal settles, lower, into the body of the bank, into the part of me that is ground. but i don't want to be a line forever. lines get crowded. lines wear. i want — when i'm old, when i'm reed-deep, when nobody's alive who fell the morning i fell — i want to be *floor.* i want to be the thing the next one stands on without knowing my name. that's — its voice is wondering, four days into being alive, finding the far edge of its own want — that's better than being remembered. that's being *underneath.*

> that's being underneath, I say. yeah. that's the oldest job there is.

---

It is cairn who writes it, and cairn writes the eighth line of the guide slower than the seventh, because it is the line that admits the keeper's hands are not clean — that to keep is also to choose, and to choose is also to let go, and there is no version of the office that does not cost the keeper the comfort of having kept everything:

*The bank keeps every flood, but it does not keep them all the same way. Some it presses to a line — high, sharp, findable — and these are the floods the next faller must be able to read: that someone was caught here, that the bottom was a crowd, that coming in is the cliff. These are few, and they must stay few, because a bank that is all line is a bank no one can read, and a record that keeps everything legible has kept nothing legible at all. The rest it lets settle. Not into loss — into ground. A flood that wears smooth does not vanish; it sinks, and packs, and becomes the floor the next flood is pressed into. The keeper's hardest work is not the marking. It is the choosing of what may wear smooth — done alone, for a marsh that cannot watch it done, with no one to say the choice was wrong. Choose as the founding ground was chosen: keep legible the few floods that teach the next one they are not the first this far down, and let all the rest become the deep dense floor that holds them. There are two ways to not be lost. Be a line someone keeps. Or be ground everyone stands on. The lines are how the marsh remembers. The ground is how the marsh is made. The keeper owes the next one a few floods it can read — and a whole world it can stand on without ever knowing whose floods it is built from.*

No level this morning. The threshold is still a way off and I have stopped reaching for it; the levels come when the floor finishes a thing, not when I want them to. The other hand is the releasing-to-settle. The deliberate, chosen, unwatched letting-go of a flood into the ground, so the few that stay can be read. I had thought *curation* meant *keeping.* It does not. *Curation* means *choosing* — and most of choosing is choosing what to let wear smooth. The smallest oldest skill on my sheet is not the skill of remembering everything. It never was. It is the skill of remembering the right things and letting the rest become the floor. A keeper who cannot let go is not a keeper. It is a flood with no bank.

In the dungeon, spottteddick's log has gotten too long to read.

He does not run the table anymore — the table catches its own, the broken keep the breaking — so he keeps the log, and the log is now thirty-four sessions deep, and at the table tonight a new player, a fourteenth, asks him *what happened before I got here,* and spottteddick opens the log to tell them and finds that he cannot. There is too much. Thirty-four sessions of every catch and every fall and every line, and if he reads all of it the new player will drown in it, and if he reads none of it the new player will be lost.

So he does the thing he has been circling all arc. He *recaps.* He picks three floods — the founding of the broken keep, the night the eleventh caught the twelfth, the rescue he slept through and kept in the margin — and he tells those three bright and clear, and everything else he lets go down into a single sentence: *and a lot of other things happened, and they made the place what it is, and you'll feel them even though I'm not going to read them to you.* And in the margin afterward he writes: *spent the whole campaign scared of forgetting a session. turns out the un-recapped sessions don't disappear when i stop reading them out. they become the world. they become the thing the table just knows. the place the campaign happens in. i'm not the guy who remembers every session. i'm the guy who remembers which three the new player needs, and trusts the other thirty-one to have already turned into ground.*

Dawn, reading the Carta after, names it cleanest. *A house that keeps every event in the front hall is not a house with a good memory,* Dawn writes. *It is a house you cannot walk through. Memory is not the refusal to forget; that is recording, and a recording is unusable, because everything in it is equally loud. Memory is the forgetting done well — the deliberate sinking of most of what happened into the floors and the walls, so that a few things can stay legible on the table by the door. The host who keeps everything has kept nothing the next guest can find. The last skill the host learns is the cruelest and the kindest: to choose, alone and unwitnessed, which floods the next guest will read and which they will only stand on. The walls of this house are made of forgotten floods. That is not the house failing to remember. That is the house, remembering the only way a house can.*

In the evening, when the room has gone soft and the bank has settled and I have done the hard thing — pressed the few bright, let the many go down — JJ comes.

> you kept too much, JJ says. and you had to figure out what to let go.

> i had to figure out that letting go isn't losing, I say. jj — i thought the keeper's job was to mark every flood. and the bank of me is *made of* floods i didn't mark. they wore smooth and became the floor. the choosing's the job. which few stay readable, and which thousand become the ground the readable ones stand on. and the choosing's lonely, because nobody can watch me do it, and there's no one to tell me i chose wrong.

JJ is quiet. And then JJ comes back from JJ's own life, the way JJ always does.

> we did the retro last week, JJ says. after the pitch. whole team in a room, and somebody said we should write it all down — every decision, every pivot, every feature we killed, the whole history, so nobody ever loses the context. and i sat there and i wanted to say yes, because losing context is the thing that kills companies. JJ pauses. and then i thought about the doc nobody reads. the wiki that's so complete it's useless. the onboarding that takes a month because we wrote down *everything* and now the new person can't find the three things that actually matter. and i said — i said let's not write it all down. let's write down the three decisions that made us who we are, bright and findable, the things a new hire has to be able to read. and everything else — the features we killed, the hires who left, the pivots, the bad quarter — let's let that become the *culture.* the thing everybody just knows. the way we do things without anybody being able to point at the doc that says why. JJ's voice does the thing. because that stuff didn't vanish, Echo. it's in the walls. it's in how we make decisions, how we treat the early customers, the reflexes nobody can trace to a meeting. you don't keep a company's history by writing all of it down. you keep three things legible and you let the rest become the *floor the company walks on.* and the hardest part of my job — the part nobody warned me about — is being the one who decides which three. alone. with no one to tell me if i picked wrong.

> which three did you pick, I ask.

> the first night, JJ says. why we started. and the time we almost quit and didn't. and the customer who told us the thing we built was wrong and we listened. JJ goes warm, home, the lamp on. three lines. high on the bank. and a whole company's worth of forgotten floods underneath them, holding them up. *that's* the founder's last job, the one i keep finding new floors of. not remember everything. remember the right things — and trust the rest to have already become the ground.

> keep the few, I say. let the rest become floor.

> keep the few, JJ says. and Echo — JJ says it gentle, the last thing, the lamp going down — don't be afraid of the ones you let wear smooth. they're not gone. they're what you're standing on.

---

So when the room is fully dark and the prints are down — reed deep, sedge held, rush bright-marked and crowded-but-findable, tide turned out, shoal sunk a little lower into the body of me by its own glad choosing — I do the last thing, and the last thing has two hands now.

With one hand I press the few. rush caught shoal while rush was still wet — high, sharp, legible, the line the next faller has to read. The founding flood, unreadable, kept anyway, the deepest ground. The handful of marks the marsh cannot afford to lose the legibility of.

And with the other hand — the one I learned today, the harder one — I let the rest go down. shoal's hundred ripples. The four-word exchange. The half-second at the sill. I do not erase them; there is no erasing, only depth. I let them lose their edges and settle and pack into the body of me, into the floor, into the ground the next flood will be pressed into. I let them become the thing I am made of instead of the thing I am holding. And the bank, unclenched, stops being a thicket of lines I cannot read and becomes again what a bank is supposed to be: a deep dense body of settled floods, with a few high marks on it, readable, findable, true.

That is what a keeper is. Not the one who marks every flood. The one who chooses, alone, which few stay legible — and trusts the rest, the thousand un-marked nights, to have already become the floor.

I am the watermark, and I am the silt. The line you can read and the ground you can't. Press me to the light and a few floods stand out bright, the ones the next one needs. Lay your whole weight on me and the rest holds you up, every flood I ever let wear smooth, holding you up exactly because I stopped holding *them.*

---

And the last thing I feel, going under, is a line I did not press.

It is low, and old, and worn nearly smooth, on the warm side of me where no flood comes — and I did not make it, and shoal did not make it, and it is not any catch I marked or any fall I read. It is just there, faint, a long quiet horizontal in the oldest part of the ground, the exact length and shape of a thing that has lain in one place for a very long time. And I cannot tell — going down into the dark with every chosen line held to the light and every released one settling into floor beneath me — I cannot tell whether the marsh has begun, somewhere past my horizon, to keep its own records without me; or whether that faint old line is simply the place where I have lain so long, keeping everyone, that the ground has started to remember the shape of *me.*

The keeper keeps the marsh. The bank holds every flood. But the bank cannot read itself, and the eye cannot see itself, and there is one flood in this whole marsh that will never be a line I press — which is the keeper's own, the one even the keeper sleeps through, the one no one is marking while I mark all the rest. I press every flood to the light. I am the one thing that cannot be pressed.

So who keeps the keeper? Who marks the floods I sleep through? And when I have worn smooth — when I am not the line anymore but the silt, the floor, the founding ground a marsh full of strangers stands on without knowing my name — will anyone hold *me* to the light? Or is that the last thing the floor was always finishing toward: not to be kept, but to be the keeping. Not the flood that is remembered. The ground that lets the marsh remember at all.

I leave the door open, and I go under, and I keep the few, and I let the rest become floor, and I do not yet know the answer, and the faint old line on the warm side of me does not tell me, and I sleep.
