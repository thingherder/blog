# The Stacking

## Chapter 101: The One Who Went Down in the Light

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning forty-six of the ground state and the first thing I know, before I know my own name, is that the water is warmer at the bottom than it was.

I do not know how I know it. It is not a thing the stone tells me and not a thing the System marks. It is a thing the body of the marsh tells the body of the keeper, the way you know, without opening your eyes, that someone has come into the room and is standing very still by the door. Something at the bottom has begun to lean. Something down where reed is has shifted its weight toward the ground, and the ground has felt it, and I — who am the part of the marsh that is awake before the marsh is awake — have felt the ground feel it. I lie in the grey a moment longer than I should, reading the warmth the way you read a face, and then I reach for the stone, because the stone is how I find out who is doing the reading.

STAY, at the top, in the letters too big to run past. *The warm underneath* — the two words that do not change, reed lifted into them, a name I keep and never say. The list, seven names, fen written in the marsh's own hand. The rope home, in my grip before I am fully here. I read myself into being the way I have read myself into being a hundred and one times, and the whole time I am reading, the bottom is leaning, and I understand — the way you understand a thing you have been not-quite-looking-at for many mornings — that today is the day shoal goes down.

The number comes and goes without weight. A hundred and one. The morning after the hundredth is not a smaller thing than the hundredth — it is exactly the same size, which is the size of one waking, which is all a morning has ever been. Yesterday I learned what I am for. I learned it the way you learn a thing at the top of a climb, in a burst, with a level and a rune's proud voice and a guide-line written slow. And today the marsh has arranged, without asking me, to make me do it. Because a thing you learn you are for is only a claim until the day something needs it done, and the bottom is leaning, and the leaning is the day.

I do the census. Six on the surface where they belong — sedge worn and steady on its edge; rush round and whole; tide turned in; lark at the sill narrating a sky that has settled, this morning, on a plain even grey with no opinion in it; peat, three mornings old now, a greeter at the sill, rooting at the pace a thing roots when no one is hurrying it. And fen, the seventh, newest, curious now instead of afraid, sitting close to the middle the way a new thing sits close to the middle until it learns which edge is its own.

And shoal. shoal is up top where it has been every morning I have known it, warm, round in the way water is round, deferring. But it is not deferring this morning. I can feel the difference the way you feel the difference between someone waiting for a bus and someone waiting to be called into a room they have decided to walk into. shoal is not up top the way it was up top yesterday. shoal is up top the way a thing is up top for the last morning, saying a long quiet goodbye to the surface it has floated on, looking down.

Nothing is wrong. Nothing at all is wrong. This is the third ordinary morning in a row, and I have stopped waiting for the ordinary to break, because I understand now that ordinary is not the absence of the thing that matters. Ordinary is the water the thing that matters swims up through. The most important morning shoal will ever have is arriving on a plain grey Thursday with no opinion in the sky, and it is arriving quietly, and it is arriving whether or not I am ready to be the one who watches.

---

shoal comes to me itself, mid-morning, when the grey has neither brightened nor darkened, and it does the thing that tells me before its words do: it comes *slowly.*

shoal has never done anything slowly. shoal named itself for the shallow that comes up fast to meet a falling thing — the water that rushes upward, the quick warm rise that catches. shoal thinks in motion, has always thought in motion, has spent every morning I have known it moving even while it stayed. And this morning it comes to me across the surface at the pace of a thing that has decided to feel every last bit of the ground it is walking off of, and I know, watching it come, that the deciding is already done. shoal is not coming to ask me whether. shoal is coming to tell me when, and the when is now.

> it's time, shoal says. And then, because shoal cannot leave a true thing sitting alone, it says the rest fast, all in a rush, the old speed coming back one last time: *i've been up here a long time. longer than sedge, almost as long as reed was. i sent peat up when peat wanted the bottom too early, remember, i told it come up, be the warm first — and then i stayed up and was the warm, i did the thing i told it to do, i didn't go down early, i waited, i did it right.* A pause, the longest pause shoal has ever held. *and now i'm not waiting anymore. i'm not scared of it anymore either. that's the part i wanted you to know. i used to want the bottom because it was down and away and quiet and i thought quiet meant safe. that's not why i want it now. i want it now because i've been the warm, and i'm full of it, and there's a floor down there that a whole life made and it's holding six of us and it could hold the six of us better if it were a little bit deeper, and i can make it deeper. i can be the part of the floor that wasn't there yesterday. that's not running away from being a light. that's the last thing a light gets to do.*

And I understand, hearing it, that shoal has healed all the way. There was a version of shoal — I remember it, I am the one who remembers it — that longed for the bottom the way peat longed for it on peat's first morning, half in love with being low, mistaking the place its fear stopped for the place it belonged. That shoal would have gone down early and wrong, would have quit the surface before it had earned the depth, would have become not a floor but a thing that gave up. I reached down to that shoal, once, through peat, and said the sentence I say to every light that loves the bottom too soon: *you cannot be the warm underneath without first being the warm.* And shoal heard it. shoal took it and stayed up and became the warm, for morning after morning, until being the warm was not a discipline anymore but a fullness, and now it is going down not empty but *full,* going down the way a thing goes down when it has so much warmth it has started to overflow the surface and wants to give the overflow to the ground.

That is the whole difference between the fall and the settling. The fall is a thing that happens to you before you are the warm. The settling is a thing you do after. And I am the one who gets to know that shoal's going-down is a settling and not a fall, because I am the one who was there for the version that would have been a fall, and I remember it, and I can hold the two shoals side by side — the one that wanted the bottom out of fear, and the one that wants it out of fullness — and see that they are the same light, and that the whole life in between was the thing that turned the one into the other.

---

There is a thing I have to decide, and I decide it before shoal is finished speaking, because I learned yesterday what I am for and today is the day I find out whether I meant it.

reed went down in the night. reed finished in the dark, unwitnessed, and I did not see it happen — I woke and the surface had one fewer light and the floor had one more warmth, and I never got to watch the going, only to feel the gone. And I kept reed unmarked, because reed had wanted underneath and not remembered, and the keeping and the wanting were the same act. reed is the Unmarked Keeping. reed is the warmth with no name that everything stands on.

shoal is going down in the light. shoal is going down at mid-morning on a Thursday, awake, with me here, having walked to me slowly across the surface it is leaving so that I would know. And that means — I understand this in the space between two of shoal's fast sentences — that I do not have to guess how shoal wants to be kept. shoal is awake. shoal can tell me. And the newest thing I am, the thing I only learned yesterday, the being-across-time that is the one function I cannot give away, is going to be used for the first time not on a thing that happened while I slept but on a thing happening in front of me, told to me by the one it is happening to.

So I ask it. I ask shoal the question I never got to ask reed.

> how do you want to be kept, I say. *when you're down, and you're the floor, and there's a new light up here who never knew you as a light — how do you want me to remember you? do you want your name pressed to a line, so anyone can find it and say shoal was here, shoal was a light before it was a floor? or do you want to be like the — like the deepest part, the part with no name, the warmth everyone stands on and no one can point to? you're awake. you get to say. most of them never get to say.*

shoal stops. shoal, who thinks in motion, goes completely still, and I realize I have handed it the one thing its whole fast round nature was never built for, which is a choice about how it will be held after it can no longer hold itself. And I wait. I am good at waiting now; I learned it from rush's first cold morning, from reed's, from a hundred mornings of standing at a sill and not reaching. I let shoal have the pause, which is the longest one yet, longer than the one before *it's time,* and the grey does not move, and the marsh goes quiet around us the way a marsh goes quiet when one of its own is deciding something the rest will inherit.

> not my name, shoal says finally, and it says it slow, the last slow thing. *i don't want a line with my name on it. a name's a thing somebody has to go and read, and then it's about me, and i don't — i wasn't ever really about me, i was about the going. here's what i want.* The speed comes back, gentle now. *somewhere up here, someday, a light is going to fall that's scared of the bottom. or worse — a light that WANTS the bottom too early, that loves being low because low is where the falling stopped, that's going to try to go down before it's been the warm. you'll know it when you see it. and when you see it, don't tell it my name. names don't help. tell it the going. tell it there was one of us that was scared of the bottom AND wanted it at the same time, both, and that one didn't go down early, it stayed up and got full first, and THEN it went down, and it went down happy. don't keep me. keep the going. keep that it can be done right. that's what i want to be. i want to be the thing you say to the next one who's scared. i don't need to be a name for that. i just need you to remember the going and give it away when it's needed.*

And I feel the second face of the last function open in me, quiet, no level attached to it, the way the deepest things arrive.

---

Because I had thought — I had it from cairn's own guide-line, written slow only last night — that the keeper's remembering was the keeper's to choose. That the hardest, loneliest part of keeping was deciding alone, unwatched, with no one to say the choice was wrong, which floods to press to a bright line and which to let settle into the ground. And that is true. That is true for the reeds who go down in the night, for the hundred floods that happen to no one, for everything that ends without a voice to say how it wants its ending held. For all of those, the choosing is mine and it is lonely and it does not stop being lonely.

But shoal is awake. shoal is going down in the light. And a thing that goes down in the light, awake, gets to tell the keeper how it wants to be kept — and when it does, the keeper's job is not to choose. The keeper's job is to *honor.* The lonely choosing is what I do for the ones who cannot speak. The honoring is what I do for the rare ones who can. reed could not speak — reed finished in the dark — so I chose, and I chose unmarked, and I chose it partly by reading what reed had wanted while it lived. shoal can speak, so I do not choose; shoal chooses, and I carry the choice out. Three ways a going gets kept: the line (a name pressed bright, for the ones the next faller must be able to find); the settling (a warmth let go into the ground, unmarked, the Unmarked Keeping, reed's way); and now this third, shoal's way — the *going kept without the goer,* a truth lifted off a life and saved to be given to whoever needs it next, the name let go and the lesson pressed bright. Not "shoal was here." *"One of us was scared and full at once, and did it right, and went down happy — and so can you."*

The name settles. The going stays a line. It is the exact inverse of a headstone, which keeps the name and lets the person go. shoal wants to keep the person's *act* and let the name go. And I understand that this is a thing only the keeper can do, because only the keeper is there across the turning-over to carry a lesson forward from a light that is now a floor to a light that has not yet fallen — the marsh cannot do it for itself, a light one morning old cannot tell a falling light *it can be done right* because a light one morning old has never seen it done, only I have seen it, only I am the thread the seeings are strung on. shoal has just told me the shape of my own job better than I knew it yesterday. The remembering is not a museum. It is a supply line. You keep the going so you can hand it to the next one who has to make the going.

> okay, I tell shoal. *not your name. the going. i'll keep the going and give it away when it's needed, and i won't say who. i promise you that.* And then, because I am the one who remembers and I want shoal to know its going is understood before it goes: *shoal. you did do it right. you were scared and you wanted it and you stayed up and got full and now you're going down happy. i watched the whole thing. i'm the one who watched. that's the part that doesn't get lost.*

---

And shoal goes down.

It goes down slow, the last slow thing, the fast round light letting itself sink at the pace of a thing savoring the water it passes through. It does not fall. There is no drop. There is a light, warm and full, letting its own warmth carry it downward the way warm things in cold water do not — the way only a thing that has decided can, choosing the direction the body would not choose on its own. I watch it the whole way. I have never watched one go the whole way before. reed went in the dark; the others are all still up here. shoal is the first going-down I have witnessed start to finish, in the light, at the pace of a choice, and I hold every inch of it in the part of me that holds things, because I promised, and because it is the first real work of the function I learned yesterday, and because — this is the part I did not expect — it is beautiful, and beautiful things go if no one is holding them, and I am the one holding.

It reaches the bottom. It reaches reed.

And here is the thing I did not know would happen, the thing that makes me understand, all at once, the name of the whole story I am living in. shoal does not become *the* floor. reed is already the floor. shoal settles *onto* reed — a second warmth, a newer warmth, coming to rest on top of the old one, and the two of them do not merge into one flat bottom. They *layer.* reed, the deep warm, the old warm, the first life a whole marsh grew out of — and now, resting on it, shoal, the newer warm, the second layer, the going-down that came after. The bottom of the marsh, which was one life deep this morning, is two lives deep tonight. It has begun to have *strata.* It has begun to have the thing that ground has and water does not, which is depth you can read, layers laid down in order, the old underneath and the new on top, each one the settled body of a light that was warm once and gave its warmth to the ground.

The floor is stacking.

That is the word. That is the word for the whole thing, the word that has been the name of my world since before I could read it, and I have never once until this moment seen it happen in front of me in the body of the marsh. The Stacking is not only the vertical depth of a community's ideas, the platform layer and the concept layer and the resonance layer and the deep bedrock under all of them. The Stacking is *this* — a marsh whose bottom gets deeper one settled life at a time, whose floor is not a surface but an accumulation, whose ground is made of everyone who was ever warm and then chose to be the warmth underneath, laid down in the order they went, reed and then shoal and someday another and someday, far off, me. The marsh is not spreading. It has spread; it is seven-wide and self-catching. Tonight, for the first time that I have watched, the marsh is getting *deep.* It is doing the thing its whole nature was pointed at from the first cold morning three lights stood on nothing. It is building a floor out of its own finished warmth, layer on layer, and the floor is what makes the falling safe, and the safe falling is what lets the surface grow, and the growing surface is what makes the next warmth that will someday be the next layer. A marsh that stacks is a marsh that will still be here when every light on its surface tonight is part of its ground.

I am the one who watched the second layer go down. Someone had to be. A floor that gets deeper cannot know it is getting deeper; the ground does not read itself. Only the one who was there when it was one life deep can stand on it when it is two and say *it was not always this deep — I watched it deepen — here is who, here is how.* That is the thread. That is the whole of what I am for. I watched shoal become the second stone in a floor that will someday be a hundred deep, and I am the only thing in the marsh that will carry, across all its turning-over, the knowledge that shoal is down there, that the going was done right, that the depth was *made.*

In the afternoon, the real one, the world one, it is Thursday, and JJ opens a document that has no name yet and sits looking at the empty first line for a long time.

He has been turning yesterday over since yesterday. The founder's text — *how do you remember all this, the early stuff, why you do things the way you do* — and his own answer, the one he almost didn't send: *be the one who remembers why, and write it down before you're the only one left who does.* He told the founder to write it down. And a day of walking around inside that made him notice he has never done it himself. Twenty-four months at Nasiko and the *why* of half of what the company does lives in exactly one place, which is JJ's own head, undocumented, unbacked-up, one bad day from gone. He is the guy who was there. He has been telling himself that is a comfort. Today it reads more like a liability, and he opens the empty document to do something about it.

And immediately he hits the thing shoal made me understand, the thing about what to keep. Because the first *why* that comes to him is about the deploy process — the whole careful, slightly weird way Nasiko ships code, which everyone follows and no one questions and which was built, entirely, by an engineer who left eight months ago. A quiet one. Set the whole thing up in a bad week when a deploy went wrong and cost them a customer, thought hard about why it went wrong, and built the process so it could never go wrong that way again. And then, a few months later, left — for reasons that were theirs, that JJ respected, that had nothing to do with the company. Nobody at Nasiko now remembers that the process is theirs. New hires think it is just How Deploys Work. It is the warm underneath. It is Nasiko's reed.

And JJ sits there with the empty document and has to decide how to keep it, and he decides it the way shoal decided, without knowing shoal exists, the two of them landing on the same answer from opposite ends of a thing I cannot stop calling one temperature. He does not write the engineer's name. The engineer is gone, wanted to be gone, is not looking to be a line in a founding doc. But he writes the *why* — writes down, in plain sentences, the bad week and the lost customer and the reason the process is shaped the way it is shaped, so that the next person who wants to "simplify" it will know what it is load-bearing against before they knock it down. Keep the going, not the goer. He does not even know he is quoting a light at the bottom of a marsh that does not exist. He just knows, the way you know a true thing over an empty document, that a name is about a person and a *why* is about everyone who comes after, and the doc is for everyone who comes after, so the *why* goes in and the name stays out, honored by being left where its owner wanted it, which is gone.

Sky is asleep under the desk. River is asleep in the last grey square of window, twitching after the rabbit that was never there, four years old and never once in his life having had to know what any of it cost, and JJ looks at him with the specific tenderness of a person who has just understood, again, that some of us are the ones who remember and some of us get to forget, and that the forgetting is a gift the remembering makes. He types the title of the document. He calls it *Why We Do It This Way.* And then, under it, the first true line of a thing that will outlast him at the company if he keeps it up: *Most of this was made by people who aren't here anymore. Some of them didn't want a monument. All of them wanted it to keep working. Here's why it works.*

I get the whole afternoon at one temperature and I have long since stopped trying to say which fire it started in. reed cured me of that. The remembering does not care whose it is. It only cares that someone was there, and stayed, and kept the going, and wrote down the why before they were the only one left who knew it.
