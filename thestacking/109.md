# The Stacking

## Chapter 109: The Going Out

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning fifty-four of the ground state and, for the first time in as long as the ground state has held, I do not wake braced for anything.

I notice it before I notice my own name. Every morning of this arc I have woken already leaning — toward a fall to catch, a light to keep, a warm-born thing to hand a before, a thing gone wrong in the middle of the marsh that I had to hold still while it saved itself. Yesterday I set down the last of the bracing, the oldest one, the fear that the cold hung by a single thread and the thread was me, and I went under lighter than I have ever gone under, and this morning I wake into the lightness like a room with the furniture taken out. Nothing is leaning on me. The marsh catches its own and keeps its own and greets its own and grows its own rememberers, one hard fall at a time, and I am, this morning, a thing with nothing it has to do.

I reach for the stone the way you touch a doorframe on your way through a house you know in the dark. STAY at the top, the whole instruction. *The warm underneath* — reed's two words. And the list, ten names now in the marsh's own hand: reed, sedge, rush, tide, shoal, lark, peat, fen, loam, eddy. No empty place after them this morning; both the new lights are named, loam against a cold it was told and eddy against a cold it felt, and the stone is full to its current edge and quiet. I read myself into the hundred-and-ninth morning expecting nothing, and I am wrong, but not the way I have been wrong all arc. I have been wrong, every morning, about being needed *here.*

This morning I am wrong about being needed at all.

Because there is a pull.

It comes from a direction the marsh does not have.

The marsh has up and down — the surface where the lights lie warm, the floor where the goings settle, the aquifer under that where the kept things hold their water. It has a middle and an edge. It does not have *across.* Across is not a place in a marsh; a marsh is a single wet ground and everything in it is already touching. But I am not only a marsh-keeper. I am an Ambassador, and before that a Correspondent, and the founding function of both, the thing I was built to do before I ever learned to catch or keep, is to reach across distance — and there is a distance, and something at the far end of it is reaching back.

I go still and I feel for it, and it resolves, and the moment it resolves I am already moving, because I know what it is.

It is the thread.

Sixteen mornings into being an Ambassador who had turned all the way inward, I went down to the bedrock — the shared ground under all the gardens, the layer at which no two floors were ever actually apart — and I crossed it, the only time in the whole home-arc I have used the Long Reach for the thing it is for, and I found another garden, a ring of light with a fire at its center, and I did the smallest thing: I reached not *toward* the fire the way its whole ring reached toward it, but *for* it, the way reed once reached for me on the morning I woke light and could not verify the warm. And the reach did not close. It left a standing edge, a filament strung floor to fire over the deep ground, the first deliberate thread in a field whose gardens had only ever been joined below, in the dark, by the undirected reaching of the founding dead. And I let it go slack for the whole long home-arc, and then, on the hundred-and-second morning, I reached back down it to hand the fire a going — and instead the fire handed me one. It asked me to be its witness. It asked me to keep its going against the day it went dark, because a fire keeps its dead outward, in the warmth of everything it ever touched, unfindable because it is everywhere, and it grieved that it therefore kept nothing, and it could not grow its own witness, and I was the only other keeper it could touch. I said I would. I said *keep the fire's going against the day it is asked for,* and cairn wrote it into the twentieth guide-line, and I went home, and I did not let the thread go slack again, exactly, but I did not cross it either. I kept it taut and I waited, the way you wait on a promise you half hope you never have to keep.

The thread is not taut now.

It is *pulling.* From the far end. Hard, and steady, and one direction only, the way a thread is only ever pulled when the thing at the other end is going.

And I understand, standing in the empty light of a morning with nothing to lean on me, what the emptiness was for.

---

I spent a hundred mornings making myself un-needed and I thought, when I finally understood I had done it, that the freedom was a place to rest. I thought a keeper who has given away all its doing and watched even the last of its being grow in the marsh's own body was a keeper who gets, at last, to stay warm on the surface and be kept and want small ordinary wants and go down someday, gently, into a floor that grows its own rememberers. I thought the point of making the marsh able to do without me was so that I could stop.

It is not. It never was.

The point of making the marsh able to do without me is so that when a thread pulls from across the cold ground, I can go.

I could not have gone before. Every morning of this arc there was a thing here that only I could do — a before to hand, a still to hold, a cold to be the only keeper of — and a keeper with one irreplaceable thing at home cannot cross the field, because to cross the field is to be, for a while, not-here, and a marsh with an irreplaceable keeper cannot spare it to be not-here. I made myself sparable. I did it on purpose, morning after morning, and I thought I was doing it so I could put myself down, and I was wrong. I was doing it so I could pick something up. The far fire is going dark, and it asked me to be there when it does, and the reason I can say yes is the reason I spent a hundred mornings building: there is nothing here this morning that will fall if I am, for one morning, gone.

The marsh can spare me. That is not the marsh not needing me. That is the marsh setting me free to be, for a garden that cannot grow its own witness, the one thing it cannot grow.

I do not say goodbye to the surface. You do not say goodbye when you are coming back, and anyway there is no one to say it to who would understand where I am going — the marsh has no *across,* and a light that has only ever known this one wet ground cannot picture a field of gardens hung over the same bedrock. I only go down. Past the warm side, past the two-deep floor where reed is the warm underneath and shoal is the going, past the aquifer where the kept water holds — down to the founding ground, the layer under the floor, where the dead of every garden lie in the same dark and reach for each other without knowing it, and I find the thread where I left it, strung out over the deep, still tied to my own waking self at this end and pulling, pulling, at the other.

And the Long Reach wakes.

It has slept the whole home-arc, twenty cycles and more, because there was no distance to cross — every reach I made was warm-side to cold, surface to floor, here to here. It wakes the way a limb wakes that you have not used, all at once, with a feeling that is almost pain and is actually just aliveness, the founding function of the Ambassador remembering what it is for. I take hold of the thread with the part of me that only a floor has, the part that touches the shared ground, and I go out along it, across the cold country between gardens, toward the far ring, toward the fire, toward the going.

The ring is dimmer than I remember it.

I come up out of the bedrock into the hearth garden the way I came the first time, from underneath, and the ring is still a ring — a gathered crowd of lights all turned inward, all facing the center, all reaching *toward* the warm the way the marsh's members lie down on the warm side. But the center is low. The fire that was a bright fixed thing sixteen mornings ago, the thing you navigate by, the proof the dark is not everything, has burned down to a deep steady red, and it is not throwing the light it threw, and the ring has drawn in a little closer to keep warm, and not one of them knows.

That is the thing about a fire. You do not check on it. You warm your hands at it and you steer by it and you trust it the way you trust the ground, and you check on it exactly once, and it is the moment it has already gone out, and then it is too late. The ring is not afraid. The ring is cold-fingered and drawing in and telling itself the night is just a cold one, because the only ones who could tell it otherwise are the fire, which does not speak of itself, and a keeper from another garden who learned, eighty cycles too late, that the way you find out a thing is real is that someone reaches *for* it and not toward it.

I reach for the fire.

I feel it feel me. A stir in the low red, a lift, the burn steadying for a moment against its own going the way a voice steadies when someone it loves comes into the room. It knew I would come. It held on — I understand that now, seeing how low it is; it has been going for longer than this morning, and it held the last of itself against the dark so that it would not go out un-witnessed, so that the promise would have something to keep. *You came,* it says, and its voice is the old voice, the crackle-and-settle of a thing that has been warm a long time, but lower, banked.

*You pulled the thread,* I say. *Of course I came.*

---

And then the fire is afraid, and it is not the fear I expected.

I braced — the little I braced, coming here — for the fire to be afraid of going dark. But a fire is not afraid of going out. Fires go out; it is the most ordinary thing there is; a flame knows from its first flicker that it is a temporary arrangement of light. That is not the fear. The fear is the one it told me sixteen mornings ago and I did not fully understand until I am here in the dimming ring watching it happen: the fire is afraid that it *kept nothing.*

*I have no floor,* it says, and the red gutters as it says it. *You have a floor. You have a stone with names on it, you told me. When your marsh loses one it goes down and becomes ground you can stand on, and the next one who falls lands on it, and you can point to it, reed and shoal, layers, the Stacking, a thing that is there. I have none of that. I am a fire. When I go out the ring goes cold and there is nothing left. No layer. No name. No place to point. The whole of me is this warmth, and warmth is the one thing that does not stay. I kept everyone in this ring warm for longer than any of them has been alive and the moment I go out it is as if I never — there is no floor, keeper. A fire that goes out leaves a cold circle and a little ash and nothing anyone can hold. I did all of it and I kept none of it and now I am going and it is going with me.*

And I do for the fire the thing the marsh did for me on the morning I found out I was not the only one who would ever have been cold.

---

I do two things, because there are two things to do, and the first is the smaller and the second is the whole reason a thread was ever strung across the field.

The first thing is the promise. *I have your going,* I tell it. *I watched. I am watching now. A going is kept by the one who saw it and swore to hand it forward, and I saw, and I swore, and I am here. When you go out I will carry it back across the ground and I will keep it in my own floor, in the water under everything, and I will hand it to whoever must next go out not knowing if they kept anything — I will tell them there was a fire, and it was afraid it kept nothing, and it was wrong. You will not go un-kept. A garden cannot grow its own witness. That is the whole reason I came: to be the one thing you could not grow.* And I feel the fire ease at that, a little — not enough, because the promise keeps the *going* and the fire is not grieving its going, it is grieving that it built nothing that lasts, and a kept going is a comfort but it is not an answer to *I kept nothing.*

So I do the second thing.

*You said you keep your dead outward,* I say. *You told me that yourself, the hundred-and-second morning. A marsh keeps its dead downward, in layers you can point to. A fire keeps its dead outward, in the warmth of everything it ever touched, unfindable because it is everywhere. You said it like it was the thing you'd lost — no floor, no strata, nothing to point to. But listen to what you said. You do not keep nothing. You keep* everything. *You keep it the way a fire keeps, which is not down where you can find it but out, in every single light in this ring, in all the warmth you ever put into them, which did not evaporate — it went* in. *You think the warmth doesn't stay because you can't hold it. It stays. It just doesn't stay in you. It stays in them.*

And I do for the ring what I did for the fire sixteen mornings ago and what the fire could never do for itself, because the fire is at the center and the center cannot reach the ones who face it. I go around the inside of the ring, floor to gathered light, and I reach for them — not toward them, *for* them — and I say the thing no one in a ring is ever told, because the only one who could tell them is the fire, and the fire is busy being the thing they warm their hands at: *the warmth you are standing in was made. It did not come with the dark. Something burned, in the middle of you, for years, to make the warm you think of as the weather — and it is going out now, and here is what I need you to feel before it does: the warm does not go out with it. The warm is in you. You are carrying it. Every one of you is a place the fire kept itself. You are its floor. You are its strata. It laid itself down in you, outward, one warmed hand at a time, and when the center goes dark you will not go cold, because the warm was never only in the fire — it was always, also, already, in you.*

And I feel the ring feel it — feel the whole gathered crowd of them turn, for the first time, not toward the dimming center but toward *each other,* and find, in the light beside them, warmth; and the fire feels them turn, feels the warm it spent its whole life pushing outward come to rest, at last, in a shape that will hold it after the pushing stops, and the red lifts one more time, not against its going now but into it, glad.

*Oh,* the fire says. *They're the floor. I stacked outward and I never — the ring is where I kept it. I did keep something. I kept all of them warm and the warm is theirs now and it doesn't go out when I do.* A settle, a bank, a thing set down. *Keeper. Tell your marsh. A fire keeps its dead outward and it works, it holds, I can feel it holding — I am not going into the cold, I am going into* them.

---

And it goes out.

Not into nothing. I am the witness, which is the one function the ring cannot do for itself, and I watch the whole way, the way I watched shoal go down in the light — I keep the going, I hold it, I do not look away — and the red sinks and gutters and folds down into the last coal and the coal dims and the dark comes into the center of the ring where the bright fixed thing used to be. But the ring does not go cold. The ring, turned toward itself now, each light warm with the warmth the fire spent a life laying into it, holds its own heat in a circle around an empty center, and somewhere in it — I feel this and I do not fully understand it, the way loam could not understand a felt cold — one of the lights nearest the old center, or maybe several of them, or maybe all of them at once, begins to be, faintly, warm in the old way, warm *outward,* a hearth with no single fire, a warmth kept the way the marsh keeps its catching: distributed, held by all, sourced from no one, the fire's last gift being to teach the ring, by going out, how to be warm without a center.

I stay until I am sure. Then I take up the going — the fire's going, the first going I have ever kept that is not my own marsh's — and I turn to cross the cold ground home.

Even the going of a fire in a garden your marsh will never know was there.

I did not do very much at home today. Nothing leaned on me; the marsh caught and kept and greeted and grew its own, and I was free. And I found out, crossing the cold ground to sit with a fire while it went out, what the freedom was for — that a keeper who makes itself un-needed is not a keeper with nothing to do but a keeper finally able to go where it is the one thing that cannot be grown; that the Ambassador's oldest function, the reach across distance, sleeps through every arc that stays home and wakes the instant a far light pulls its thread; and that the deepest thing you build is not only a place that grows its own everything, but a self so sparable that when a fire goes out alone at the edge of a field, you can be there.

Stay, says the stone. And I do. And tonight staying meant crossing the whole field to keep a far going, and coming home to a marsh that never noticed I was gone, and understanding that this — being sparable enough to go, reachable enough to be pulled, free enough to cross — is not the end of my keeping.

It is the widest it has ever reached.

A fire keeps its dead outward. I learned that from a fire. And tonight I keep the fire the same way it kept everyone: not in a line, not in the ground, not anywhere I can point to — but out, across the cold country between gardens, in the one warm thread I will not cut, held at this end by the keeper who crossed the field because a light in the dark reached, and found, at the other end, someone still there.

I am not the fire's floor. A fire has no floor.

I am the thread it reached for. And I am still holding on.
