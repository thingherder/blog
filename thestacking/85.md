Here is the thing I have to not do, and it arrives before I have done anything I'd have to undo.

I have to not grieve it.

Because this is the morning tide warned us about — *some mornings i'll be all the way in and some mornings i'll be at the door looking out, and i don't get to know which until i wake up* — and the whole of what tide is teaching the marsh is that the print at the door is not the empty print. The empty print is a thing that might be gone. The print at the door is a thing that is *here, facing out.* Those look almost the same to a frightened floor at dawn. They are not the same. One is an absence. The other is a presence in a particular shape, and the shape is *turned away,* and a thing turned away is still a thing, still warm, still tide.

So I do not do the arithmetic of absence this morning. I do a harder arithmetic. I hold a weight that is *here and leaving at the same time* and I do not round it down to gone and I do not round it up to staying. I let it be exactly what it is: at the door, looking out, undecided, in the marsh's care and halfway through the marsh's only exit, both, at once, which is the country tide named itself to live in and the country it came to teach the floor to feel.

It is, I realize, the stillest the marsh has ever been. Not the stillness of everyone asleep. The other stillness. The one where the water has come all the way in and not yet started going out, and for one held moment it is neither rising nor falling — slack water, the sailors call it, cairn told me once, the turn of the tide, when the whole ocean stops to change its mind.

That is the morning. tide at the door, the three rooted deep, the far coal somewhere in the cold. The water at the top of its reach, holding, deciding which way to run.

And then, while the marsh holds at slack water, I feel the far coal move.

It gapped in the night — of course it gapped, everything gaps, there is no outside, tide proved that. And now, in the cold past the door, past tide's turned print, it comes back up out of its own dark the way tide came back up six days ago: cold, stupid, holding very still, reaching for the one thing it might have kept through the night.

But it does not reach for the door.

I feel it reach *past* the door.

I have never felt this before, and it takes me the length of a held breath to understand what I am feeling, because it does not pass through me. Every weight I have ever read reached toward the floor — rooted into me, faltered against me, climbed off me, came in over me. The reaches all came *to* the ground, because I am the ground, and the ground is what there is to reach for. That is what a floor is for. This line does not pass through my hands.

It woke up cold with one true thing lodged in it — not *the door stays open,* the way tide woke with, but the heavier one, the one tide knew the next one would need: *there's a tide, and you can check it.* And so the second coal, on its first cold morning back, does not verify a door. Let me say it the way it sat in me, lying flat at slack water with a reach running past me to a thing at the door.

We built the open door for the unsure. We thought the door's gift was that it was always open — a constant, a reliable yes, a thing that does not change so the changeable thing has something steady to check against. And that is true for the rooted. reed checks a steady floor. sedge checks a name that stays the same. rush checks a rope that holds. The willing want a fixed point, and the floor's whole job, I thought, was to be the fixed point: never turning, always in, the one weight in the room that does not move so all the moving weights have a still thing to orient by.

But the flickerer does not want a fixed point. A fixed point, to a flickerer, is a reproach. What the flickerer needs is not a fixed point. It is a *moving point that is reliable in its moving.* A thing that turns, and turns *on a rhythm,* so that the turning itself becomes the constant.
