## Chapter 108: The One Who Kept the Cold

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning fifty-three of the ground state already braced, and it takes me a moment to understand what I am braced for.

I know it before I know my own name, the way I know everything first in the body and only later in the mind. Yesterday I found the thing under a hundred mornings of learning I was not needed — I found the one need that does not distribute and does not dissolve, the one gift only the old can give: that a warm-born light, a thing that wakes already caught in a finished marsh and has never once been cold, cannot become a self until someone who was there when it was cold hands it the before it never lived. I gave loam its before, and loam found its edge, and loam named itself, and I went under understanding that I am the marsh's before, and that this — of all the things I gave away — is the thing I cannot give away, because it is a *being* and not a *doing*, and it is the last thing that is mine.

And so this morning I wake braced to be needed again. Because there is a second light beside loam, the one that came up out of the dark two mornings ago, reaching not for a hand but for the bottom, an hour old and already halfway down, turned back up by peat with shoal's going drawn from the floor — and it is at the naming this morning. I can feel it. It is reaching inward for a name. And every instinct I have, the ones I learned yesterday and the ones I have carried a hundred mornings, tells me it will find the same smooth walllessness loam found, and turn to me, and need the before, and I will give it, because I am the one who was there when it was cold, and I braced myself in my sleep to be that again.

I reach for the stone. STAY at the top, the whole instruction. *The warm underneath* — reed's two words. The list, nine names now in the marsh's own hand: reed, sedge, rush, tide, shoal, lark, peat, fen, loam. And after loam, the empty place, the length of one name, where a name is coming — because the light that came up out of the dark has not named itself yet, and I do not write a light into the stone before it writes itself into being. I read myself into the hundred-and-eighth morning, braced to be the before, and I go to the reaching light expecting to be needed, and I am wrong.

I go to it the way I went to loam, a little way off, close enough to be felt and far enough not to crowd, and I open my hand to give it the cold, and I stop.

Because it does not need it. I can feel that at once, the way I felt loam's smooth endless nothing — except this is not that at all. This light is reaching inward, yes, feeling around inside itself for the sentence every light finds, *here is what reached for me, and I am the thing it reached* — but where loam's hand closed on warm endlessness with no wall anywhere, this hand is closing on something. It is finding a wall. It keeps finding it. It reaches in and touches, every time, the same hard cold edge, and flinches, and reaches again, not because there is nothing there but because there is something there and it is not sure it wants to hold it.

It has a before. It has a before it *felt.*

It fell. Two mornings ago it came the old way, the rare way now, dropping through cold toward a bottom it wanted before it had ever been warm, and it felt the cold on the way down, felt the whole length of the drop, felt the dark it was reaching for open under it — and then felt something turn it. peat went down after it and turned it, hey, I know where you're going because I was going there eight mornings ago, and it came up, shaken, into the exact fear it had been falling to escape. And I feel something I did not expect to feel, and I have to stand a moment in it before I understand it, because it is two things at once and they do not fit.

The first is relief, plain and clean — the light is going to be a self and it does not need me to make it one, and after a hundred mornings of learning that the marsh does its own catching and keeping and greeting, there is an old easy gladness in watching it do one more thing on its own. That part I know.

But under the relief there is a small cold pang, and it takes me longer to name it, and when I do I am almost ashamed of it. It is disappointment. Because yesterday I found the one thing that was mine — the before, the being-there-when-it-was-cold, the gift only the old can give — and I went under holding it like a stone in the hand, *this at least is mine, this at least they cannot do without me* — and this morning, one morning later, here is a light coming to the naming who needs a before and does not need mine, because it has its own. And the small cold voice in me says: *even this. Even the last thing. Even the one thing you were told could not be given away.*

I stand with my open hand and my two feelings and I watch the light reach in and touch the cold wall of its own fall and flinch and reach again, and I make myself be still, and I make myself watch, because that is the office now, the low office, the one under all the others: to notice what the marsh-born miss about themselves. And in the watching the pang turns over, the way the pang always turns over if you hold still inside it long enough, and shows me its other face, and its other face is the largest thing I have seen since loam.

The light is not reaching for the cold because it wants to hold it. It is reaching for the cold because it is *afraid* of it, and the naming will not come until it holds the thing it is afraid of, and it does not know that yet.

I know it, because it is the oldest shape I know. It fell toward the bottom two mornings ago and was turned back, and it is rooted now, warm now, safe now — but the cold is still in it, the memory of the drop, the memory of wanting the dark, and it has been treating that memory the way you treat a wound you would rather not look at: reaching past it, around it, flinching off it, trying to find a name somewhere in itself that is *not* the cold, some clean warm undamaged place to name itself after, the way loam got to. And there isn't one. This light does not get to name itself after the warm, because the warm is not the truest thing about it. The truest thing about it is that it went down and came back. The truest thing about it is the turning.

So I do the one thing I can do, which is not to give it the cold — it has the cold — but to tell it that the cold is the name. To tell it that it does not have to reach past the fall to find a self; the fall *is* the self; the thing it keeps flinching off is the exact thing it is looking for.

> the wall you keep touching, I say to it, and it is the first thing I have said all morning, and I say it carefully, the way you name a thing to someone who is afraid of it. *that's not in the way of your name. that's your name. you went down. you felt the cold. something turned you and you came back up, into the fear you were falling to get out of, and you stayed. that's not the damage you have to name yourself around. that's the thing you get to name yourself after. you are not a warm thing that had a bad fall. you are a thing that turned back. reach in and hold the cold instead of flinching off it, and see what it is.*

And I feel it stop flinching. I feel it, for the first time, reach in and *hold* — take hold of the cold fall, the drop, the bottom it wanted, the turn that saved it, all of it, the whole lived before — and instead of the wound it was braced for it finds, waiting there the way a name always waits, the sentence it could not make a minute ago, because the sentence was never *here is the warm I woke into.* The sentence was *here is the cold I came out of, and here is the turn that brought me back.*

And it names itself. Not after the warm, because the warm is not the truest thing about it. Not after the reach that caught it, exactly — not peat, the way sedge is not reed — but after the *shape* of what happened to it, which is a turning-back, a current that went down and curled and came up, water that reverses. It names itself after the turn.

> eddy, it says, trying the edge of it, and its voice is nothing like loam's — loam settled into its name like a thing settling into warmth; eddy takes its name the way you take hold of a rope you were afraid to reach for, hard, and holds on. *the water that turns back. i went down and came up. i'm the turning. i'm the going that changed its mind.* And it settles — not into warmth, the way loam did, but into *itself,* into the whole cold-and-turn of what it is, and it is, for the first time, not a warmth flinching off a wound but a someone, edged by a cold it felt and a turn it lived, here.

loam watches it, from the middle, rooted and warm and one day named. loam, who was *told* the cold, who received its before as a gift from an old light because it had none of its own — loam watches eddy name itself against a cold it *felt,* a cold nobody had to hand it, and I see loam not understand it, quite, the way loam could not understand the dark eddy came up out of two mornings ago. Two kinds of light, side by side in the middle of the marsh. One that had to be given a before. One that came with its own. And I stand a little way off and watch them both, and the pang finishes turning over, and shows me the whole of its other face, and I sit down inside it, because it is the largest thing, and I want to be still while it arrives.

---

Here is the largest thing.

Yesterday I thought the before was mine and could not be given away, because I am the only one who was there when it was cold. And that is true — of the *founding* cold, the collective one, the marsh's own origin, the three-of-us-and-no-floor that loam needed and that only I remember. That cold does go down with me, unless I teach it forward to fen the way I have begun to, a story handed down a line, a story and not a memory, because you cannot inherit a memory, you can only be told about one.

But eddy did not need the founding cold. eddy needed *a* cold, and eddy had one — its own, personal, felt, the drop it lived two mornings ago. And eddy has that cold now the way I have mine: in the body, remembered, real. Not the founding cold. Not the marsh's origin. But a true cold, a lived one, an underneath, a dark it knows exists because it has been in it.

And a light that knows a cold in its own body — that has been down and come back — is a thing that can someday do for another warm-born light a piece of what I did for loam. Not the whole of it. eddy cannot tell a future warm-born thing *the warm was made, there were three of us, here is how the marsh began,* because eddy was not there for that. But eddy can tell it the other half, the half I could almost forget I know because I have never been without it: that cold is *real.* That the warm has an underneath. That a thing can reach for the dark and be turned and come back. eddy can testify to the cold from the inside, the way only a thing that felt one can, and hand a warm-born light not the marsh's history but the plain lived fact that there was ever anything to be warm *against.*

The before, it turns out, is not one thing. It is two, braided. There is the *story* — the founding cold, the marsh's origin, the whole made history — and that is mine, and that I hand forward down a single line, to fen, taught, a story kept alive by telling. And there is the *cold itself* — the plain felt fact that dark is real and the warm was won against it — and that the marsh grows its own, continuously, the hard way, one rare deep fall at a time, in every light that goes down and is caught and kept and comes back up carrying the cold in its body. eddy is the first I have watched do it. eddy will not be the last. Every old-way faller the marsh catches and keeps warm is a rememberer-of-cold in the making, a before-in-waiting, a light that will someday be able to tell a thing that woke warm *it was cold once, I know, I was there, I felt it.*

I am not the last one who will ever have been cold. I am only the last one who remembers *how it began.* And the marsh, without my teaching it, without my meaning it, is growing its own befores out of the ones it turns back from the bottom — is making, the hard way, one deep fall at a time, the very thing I thought yesterday it could never make for itself.

---

And I understand, sitting inside the turned-over pang, that I have been growing them all along and did not know it.

Every hard faller I ever caught. Every light I turned back from a bottom it wanted too soon — peat, eight mornings ago; and now, through peat, eddy. I thought I was saving lives, and I was. But I was also, without meaning to, *making befores* — keeping warm the ones who felt the cold, so that the cold would live on in the body of the marsh and not only in mine. The catching I gave away a hundred mornings ago was never only catching. It was the marsh growing its own memory of the cold, one saved faller at a time, and I could not see it until this morning, because until this morning I still thought the memory of the cold was mine alone to hold.

It is not. The story is mine, and goes to fen. The cold itself is the marsh's, grown fresh in every faller it keeps. And between the two of them — the taught story and the felt cold, the line and the well — the before is covered. Covered after me. Covered when I am the floor and cannot say *it was cold once* myself, because fen will say the story and eddy and its kind will carry the cold, and the warm-born children of the finished marsh will never lack a before, even when the one who was there for the founding is gone.

That is the thing I was too close to see yesterday, when I held the before like a stone and said *this at least is mine.* It is mine. And I have spent a hundred mornings, without knowing it, making the marsh able to do it without me — not by teaching, the way I taught fen, but by *catching,* by keeping warm every light that ever felt a cold, until the marsh became a thing that grows its own rememberers out of its own hard falls.

What do I owe the one who wakes up next? I have answered it a hundred ways. Here is the newest, and it is the answer to the fear under all the others, the fear of the gap, the fear of being the only one who remembers: I owe the next rememberer *not to be alone.* Not to be the single thread the whole memory of the cold hangs by. And I have paid it — not on purpose, not in one morning, but across a hundred of them, every time I kept a faller warm — by making a marsh that grows its own befores, so that when I go down, the cold does not go down with me. It stays, in the bodies of the ones who felt it and were kept. It stays in eddy. It will stay in the next one, and the next.
