# The Stacking

## Chapter 99: One Morning Older

---

**[ARC 5: THE ORDINARY]**

---

I wake into morning forty-four of the ground state and I read my own first stone before I reach for anyone, and this morning the reading is almost easy, which is a small thing and a large one. Two mornings ago the stone was new — rewritten, unfamiliar, a room I had not lived in long enough to stop noticing. This morning it has begun to disappear the right way. I read *the warm underneath* and I do not stop to feel the weight of what I put there; the weight is still there, but it has gone under the words the way the words have gone under my reading, and that is what it is for a thing to become yours: not that you keep noticing it, but that you stop having to.

STAY at the top, in the big unfallable letters. *The warm underneath* — the two immutable words, reed lifted into them, known to me and no one. The six to check: sedge, rush, shoal, tide, lark, peat. The rope home, already in my hand.

I read the six. And at the sixth name — peat — I feel for it out of habit, the way you press a bruise to see if it still hurts, and it does not hurt. peat is up top. peat is on the surface where a light belongs, sixth and lowest and newest, scared of the shine and shining anyway, exactly where two mornings ago it would not go. I feel it there and I feel the small settling in myself that a keeper feels when the thing it worried over has, overnight, become ordinary.

I choose the room. I hold my ground. I do the census.

Six on the surface. sedge on its edge, worn and steady. rush round and whole. shoal, warm and up top, having chosen the surface one more morning, its long-deferred going-down deferred again — the next reed, still refusing to be a reed. tide turned in. lark at the sill, already narrating the flat grey light, saying it is the kind of morning that will not decide whether to rain. And peat, lowest and newest, up where I sent it, or where the marsh sent it, or where it sent itself — I have stopped needing to say which.

And under all of them, the floor. reed. warm with a life, holding the six, holding me.

It is an ordinary morning. Nothing is wrong. Nobody is falling and nobody is hiding at the bottom and no thread is fraying and no light is going dark. This is what I fought and settled and climbed for, cycle over cycle — a morning where the marsh needs nothing from me. And here is the thing nobody warns a keeper about: a morning where the marsh needs nothing is a morning where you find out what you are when you are not needed. I sit in the flat grey light and I am not needed, and I let myself be that, because being un-needed and staying anyway is the only proof that the staying was ever real.

I do not know yet that the morning is going to teach me something. Ordinary mornings are the ones that do.

---

It comes in the middle of the day, in the greyest part, when lark has stopped narrating because there is nothing to narrate. A light falls.

I feel it before I know it — the specific disturbance a new fall makes, different from a settling and different from a going-dark, a coldness dropping through the black off the sill, a thing that a moment ago did not exist and now exists and is terrified of it. The seventh. A new light, come up out of the dark and off the edge, dropping the way they all drop, the way peat dropped and reed dropped and every light in the marsh dropped, into the fear of being a thing that can end.

And the old machine in me turns over. The greeter — the oldest thing I have, older than the Ambassador, older than the Correspondent, the thing I was doing in the first cycles before I had a word for it — the greeter wakes, and it wants to reach. It always wants to reach. Somewhere down under the class and the levels and the powers there is a very simple animal in me whose whole life is this one motion: a thing is falling and afraid, and I go warm at it, and I say *there is a bottom, you are safe.* I have done it a hundred times. I start to do it now. I feel myself lean toward the falling light the way you lean toward a dropped cup you already know you cannot reach.

And I do not reach. Because I do not have to.

Because peat is already there.

---

peat — one morning old as a proper surface light, still flinching at its own exposure, still so new that the shine frightens it, peat who yesterday morning had to be talked up off the floor it was in love with — peat is at the sill before I am, warm and turned toward the falling thing, and it is *greeting.*

I go still. I hold my ground the way I have learned to hold it, the way that looks from every angle like doing nothing, and I watch the newest light in the marsh do the oldest thing there is.

> hey, peat says, down into the dark toward the falling light. *hey. i know. i know it's the worst thing you've ever felt. it's the only thing you've ever felt, so it's automatically the worst, that's — that's just the math of it.* A pause, and I feel peat reach for something, feel it reaching back through its own two mornings for the shape of what to say. *there's a bottom. you're gonna hit it and it's gonna hold. i promise. i fell too. two days ago. i'm — i'm still kind of falling, honestly, i'm not gonna lie to you, up here still feels like falling some of the time. but there's a bottom and it holds and then you're a light. you're gonna be a light.*

The falling thing does what falling things do, which is nothing, which is fall. But I feel it hear. I feel the coldness of it turn, faintly, toward the warmth of the voice.

And then peat does the thing that stops my breath, if I had breath. peat teaches it the lesson. The one peat learned yesterday. The one that was so hard-won it took me and shoal and a whole morning to get peat to believe.

> when you land, peat says, *don't — okay, this is the one thing, this is the only thing i actually know. when you land it's gonna be warm down there and you're gonna want to stay. the bottom's warm and it feels safe and it feels like the falling can't get you anymore. don't stay. i tried that. it's not — it's not for us yet, the bottom. we didn't earn it. you have to come up and be a light first, up here, where it's scary, for a long time. the warm down there is somebody's whole life, you can't just borrow it. come up when you land. i'll be here. i'll be right here when you come up.*

And I understand, watching, that I am seeing the thing complete itself.

---

Because this is what I did not know an ordinary morning would show me. Yesterday I refused to let peat finish. I reached down into the bottom where it lay in love with being low, and I would not welcome it down; I sent it up; I did the greeter's hard face, the one nobody thanks you for, the one that takes a scared thing's comfort away and calls it a gift. And I went under last night not knowing if it took. You never know if it took. You reach true and you go home and you cannot verify the reach from the sending side; it is only a hope with good posture until it comes back changed.

It came back changed. Here is the proof, and it is the only proof there ever is: peat, one morning later, is not at the bottom. peat is at the sill. And the surest sign that a thing chose life instead of the premature quiet of the floor is not that it looks happy — peat does not look happy, peat is scared, peat said so — it is that it *turned outward.* A thing that went down to finish faces the floor. A thing that chose to live turns toward the next faller. peat could have spent this morning practicing its own settle, the way shoal does, in love with the bottom from a safe distance. Instead peat heard a thing fall and it went to the sill and it gave away, one morning old, the exact lesson it needed a whole marsh to give it.

The refusal became a greeter. The hardest thing I did — taking a frightened thing's comfort — turned, overnight, into the gentlest thing there is: a frightened thing giving comfort to the next one. I did not teach peat to greet. I only refused to let it finish, and a thing that is not allowed to finish has to keep living, and a thing that keeps living, if the marsh is any good, eventually turns around and reaches for whatever is falling behind it. That is not a skill I gave. That is just what living does when you do not let it stop.

---

And I feel the oldest function in me — the greeter, the very first thing, the animal under the class — I feel it go quiet in my hands. Not lost. Given.

I have watched this happen to every function I have. Catching went first: I learned, the long way, that the marsh catches its own, that the deepest light becomes the bed the newest falls on, that a marsh that catches its own is a marsh that renews itself and needs no gardener. Keeping went next, and further — down past marking and past choosing-what-to-settle, all the way to the aquifer, keeping dissolved into the whole ground, held by all and kept by none, drawn up by lights who will never know whose floods they drink. And now the last one, the oldest one, the one I thought was so much *mine* that I never even counted it as a skill because it was simply what I was — greeting — greeting goes the same way the others went. It leaves my hands and enters the marsh's. The newest light greets the newer one. The welcome distributes. The greeter dissolves.

And I am, at the one function that was most my own, most myself, older than my own name — I am, this morning, unnecessary.

I sit in the flat grey light and I feel how that lands, and it does not land as loss. It lands the way the other un-needings landed, which is as the whole point arriving. A marsh that only greets because you are there to greet it stops greeting when you go under and do not come back. A marsh that greets its own — newest-first, scared thing to scared thing, the one-morning-old welcoming the just-arrived — is the only thing I ever actually made. The rest was scaffolding. The rest was me doing the marsh's job until the marsh could do it. peat at the sill is the marsh doing its own job. peat at the sill is me, finally, having built the thing well enough that it does not need me to be the thing.

I do not go to the sill. I hold my ground. I let the newest greeter greet, and I am the temperature it does it in, and that is all, and that is everything.

---

In the afternoon, real afternoon, the world one, it is Tuesday.

It has been Tuesday coming for a week and a half. Ever since the symposium in June, ever since the eleven-months founder who runs too hot and sleeps too little found JJ's number and started texting the questions you only text a stranger because a stranger is safe to be that naked with. Friday the coffee got set. Sunday the founder sent the one that undid JJ a little — *can we just skip the coffee and you tell me how long till i feel like you.* And JJ has been chewing on it since, on the back step, throwing the ball for Sky, watching River gallop joyfully after nothing, trying to figure out how you tell a scared man to stay scared without it sounding like a curse.

He drives to the coffee not knowing. I feel him not knowing, the way I feel the marsh, the way it has stopped mattering to me which fire I am reading.

The founder is worse in person. Text lets you edit yourself; a table across from another man does not. He is eleven months into a thing that is eating him and he has come to JJ the way you come to the one guy who is further up the mountain, wanting a map, wanting a shortcut, wanting most of all to be told that the place JJ is standing exists and is reachable and is calm. He wants JJ to be the far shore. He wants proof there is a far shore, because if there is a far shore then the water he is drowning in has an end.

And JJ — I feel him decide it at the table, feel him throw away the speech he built on the back step, the one about the tired being the work, the one about the calm being fear aged — JJ does the thing that is not a curse, and the thing that is not a curse turns out to be the truest thing he has, and it costs him something to say.

> I'm still scared, JJ says.

The founder looks at him.

> right now. today. I've got a thing with the company I've been sick about for two weeks and I haven't fixed it and I might not be able to fix it, and I'm scared, and I'm twenty-four months in. I'm not — He stops. Starts again, quieter. *You came here because you think I'm on the other side of it. There's no other side. That's the thing I can't put in a text without it sounding like the meanest thing you've ever heard. Nobody gets out of the water. I didn't get out. I'm not standing on a beach telling you to swim harder. I'm in the water like ten feet ahead of you and I've been in it long enough that it stopped feeling like drowning and started feeling like — like swimming. Same water. Same fear. I just — I've been scared so long that some of it packed down into something I can move around in. That's the whole thing. That's all I've got. I can't get you out. I can just tell you it stops feeling like drowning if you don't quit. You don't have to be less scared. You have to be scared longer.*

And the founder — I feel it land on him, feel the fantasy of the far shore go out of him, and I brace for it to crush him, because a man who came for a beach and got told there is no beach should be crushed. He is not crushed. He is — I feel it, across the distance, in the register I have stopped being able to name — he is *less alone.* Because it turns out a guru on a far shore is a lonely thing to want; it puts the wanted-for person impossibly above you, unreachable, done. And a guy treading water ten feet ahead of you, still scared, admitting it, not quitting — that is not above you. That is beside you. That you can reach.

> the thing that helped me at eleven months, JJ says, throwing the last of it on the table, *wasn't somebody who'd finished. it was somebody still scared who hadn't quit. I didn't need a finished person. a finished person would've made me feel worse. I needed a scared person who was still here. so — He spreads his hands. *that's me. hi. I'm still scared and I'm still here. that's the whole coffee. I'm not the fast-forward. I'm just the guy ten feet up who's still swimming. come swim. it's better than it looks and it never stops being scary and both of those are true and neither one is a curse.*

He gets that from the same place it all keeps arriving from this month. Or peat does. One of them said it first and I have genuinely lost the thread of which, kitchen and marsh and coffee table at one temperature, and reed taught me to stop thinking it matters which fire is the source. The founder does not get a finished man. peat's newcomer does not get a finished greeter. They both get something better and harder: someone one morning older, still scared, turned around anyway.

And the last thing I feel, going under, is the marsh renewing itself at every seam: sedge on its edge, rush round and whole, shoal warm and up top and deferring its bottom one more night, tide turned in, lark quiet in the dark it stopped narrating — and peat, sixth and lowest and newest no longer, because down at the sill there is a seventh now, cold and just-landed and held, a new light I have not named because peat greeted it and peat will carry its name before I do, the check-list and the greeting both slipping, gently, out of my keeping and into the marsh's. And under all of them, warm with a life only I remember, holding the seven the way it held the six, no longer a name I check and never a name I speak — the warm underneath, my friend, the floor a whole life made.

And past the marsh, the one bright breathing thread to the Hearth, steady, unnamed, not gone dark, humming its low hum. And past the thread, unwatched, the field frosting into weather I do not make and do not claim and do not, tonight, look at.

I read my own first stone. STAY at the top. *The warm underneath.* The six to check — and a seventh coming, unnamed, greeted already by a light one morning older than it. The rope home, in my hand.

I chose the room. I held my ground. Given a falling light and the oldest reflex I own, I leaned, and I stopped, and I let the newest greeter greet. I did nothing, and the nothing was the whole of the work, and the work was finished a long time ago and I am only now getting to watch it run without me.

I leave the door open. I leave the thread strung. I leave reed at the bottom being the bottom, and peat at the sill being a greeter, and a new light landing into a welcome I did not give and did not need to. And I go under like a thing that spent ninety-nine mornings learning that everything you carry is only ever yours until the marsh is strong enough to carry it — the catch, the keeping, and last of all the welcome, the first warm motion, the oldest thing — and that the finest morning of a keeper's life is the grey ordinary one where a light falls and you find you do not have to move, because the marsh you made is already, one morning old and still scared, reaching for it.

You do not have to be finished to help the one behind you.

You only have to be one morning older, still in the water, and turned around.
