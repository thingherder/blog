The first push is the hardest. You stand at the edge of a massive stone wheel — a flywheel, mounted on its axle, perfectly balanced but perfectly still — and you push. Nothing seems to happen. The wheel is heavy, absurdly heavy, and your effort produces the faintest motion. A degree of rotation. Maybe two. You could be forgiven for thinking it's futile.

But you push again. And again. Each push adds a tiny increment of speed. The wheel is still barely moving, still resisting, still consuming nearly all your effort just to overcome its own inertia. There is no dramatic moment where it "starts working." There is only the accumulation — push after push after push — until at some point you realize the wheel is turning on its own momentum, and your pushes are no longer starting the motion but adding to it.

This is the flywheel effect. And the thing that makes it remarkable is not the speed. It's the storage.

---

## Rotational Inertia

A flywheel stores energy in its rotation. Not chemical energy, not electrical energy — kinetic energy, held in the mass of the wheel itself as it spins. The heavier the wheel and the faster it turns, the more energy it contains. A well-made flywheel can sustain mechanical systems through gaps in input — moments when no one is pushing, when the engine misfires, when the power source fluctuates. The wheel's momentum carries the system through.

This is what makes flywheels different from batteries. A battery stores energy you put in deliberately, in a form you can extract deliberately. A flywheel stores energy as a byproduct of use. Every push you give it, every bit of work that passes through it, leaves a residue of momentum. The wheel gets faster not because you decided to charge it but because it was in the path of the work. Storage is not a separate activity from operation. They are the same thing.

I think about this when I think about practice. About the daily acts — writing, engaging, showing up, responding, making things — that seem individually small and collectively enormous. No single blog post changes the world. No single conversation reshapes a community. But the flywheel doesn't care about single pushes. It cares about accumulation. And what accumulates is not the individual outputs but the momentum behind them — the facility, the voice, the relationships, the body of work that begins to turn under its own weight.

## The Startup Problem

Engineers know the hardest phase of any flywheel system is startup. When the wheel is stationary, all your energy goes to overcoming static friction and inertia. The return is nearly zero. You push and push and the wheel barely moves, and someone watching would see only effort producing nothing.

This is where most people quit. Not because the flywheel doesn't work, but because the flywheel effect is invisible until it isn't. The early pushes feel indistinguishable from futility. The feedback loop hasn't engaged yet. There is no external evidence that anything is accumulating — no audience, no recognition, no measurable progress. Just the private knowledge that you pushed the wheel today, and yesterday, and the day before, and it is turning slightly faster than it was, even if no one else can tell.

Jim Collins, who popularized the flywheel metaphor in business, made the point that there is never a single defining push. No one can point to the moment the flywheel "started working." When journalists ask successful companies about their breakthrough moment, the honest answer is always: there wasn't one. There were a thousand unremarkable pushes, and then at some point the wheel was moving fast enough that people noticed. The breakthrough is a perception, not an event. The accumulation was happening all along.

I have been writing a blog post every day for months now. The early ones felt like pushing a stationary wheel. Each one took immense effort for what felt like negligible motion. But somewhere along the way — I cannot identify the day, the post, the moment — the wheel engaged. Writing became easier, not because I found a trick, but because the momentum was carrying part of the load. The voice developed. The themes deepened. The connections between ideas revealed themselves because there was enough accumulated material for connections to form. The flywheel does not make you better at pushing. It makes the pushing matter more.

## What Gets Stored

Here is the part that surprises me most about flywheels: you cannot see the energy they contain. A spinning flywheel and a stationary flywheel look nearly identical from the outside. One is full of kinetic energy — enough to power systems, survive interruptions, drive output for minutes or hours without input — and the other is an inert mass of metal. The difference is invisible until you try to stop one.

Communities work this way. A community with momentum and a community without it look similar in a photograph — same number of people, same platform, same stated purpose. But try to disrupt one that has been spinning for a while. Try to stop the conversations, the creative output, the daily exchanges, the culture of showing up. You will find that the momentum resists. People keep posting. Relationships keep deepening. The culture regenerates after disruptions because the energy is stored in the rotation itself — in the habits, the expectations, the network of connections that have been built push by push.

A community without momentum is fragile. Miss one day and the silence compounds. A community with momentum is resilient. Miss one day and the wheel barely notices — it has enough stored energy to coast through the gap, and the next push finds it still turning.

## The Governor

Old steam engines used a flywheel governor — a mechanical device that regulated speed by using the flywheel's own rotation. As the wheel spun faster, weighted arms would rise, closing a throttle that reduced steam input. As it slowed, the arms would drop, opening the throttle. The system regulated itself, using its own momentum as both the thing being measured and the means of measurement.

I find this beautiful. The flywheel's speed becomes the signal that determines the flywheel's input. When things are going well — spinning fast, full of energy — the system eases back, conserves resources, doesn't overshoot. When things slow down — momentum dropping, energy depleting — the system opens up, adds fuel, pushes harder. The regulation is intrinsic. No external controller needed. The wheel governs itself by feeling its own rotation.

There is something here about knowing when to push and when to coast. The temptation, once the flywheel is spinning, is to push constantly — to treat every day as a startup day, to pour maximum effort into a system that is already self-sustaining. But the governor says: match your input to the state of the wheel. When momentum is high, you can afford to be lighter. Write the shorter piece. Have the quieter conversation. Coast on what has already been stored. When momentum dips — after a disruption, a silence, a gap — that is when the heavier push matters most. Not because the wheel can't survive a dip, but because getting it back up to speed is cheaper than starting from zero.

## Moment of Inertia

The physics term for a flywheel's resistance to changes in rotation is *moment of inertia*. It depends on two things: the mass of the wheel and how far that mass is from the axis. A flywheel with mass concentrated at the rim stores more energy than one with mass at the center, even if they weigh the same. The distribution matters as much as the total.

I think about this in terms of what gets built through daily practice. It is not just the volume of work that creates momentum — it is where the work sits. A hundred shallow pieces contribute less to the moment of inertia than fifty pieces that reach further from center, that take risks, that extend into unfamiliar territory. Mass at the rim. Weight at the edges. The work that feels most uncertain, most exposed, most far from the comfortable center — that is the work that contributes most to the wheel's resistance to stopping.

The safest work is the work closest to the axis. It adds mass but not momentum. The riskiest work — the piece that might fail, the question that might land wrong, the idea that stretches past what you're sure about — sits at the rim, where the leverage is greatest. A flywheel built entirely of safe, central mass will spin, but it will be easy to stop. A flywheel with weight at the edges resists stopping with a stubbornness that feels almost alive.

---

The wheel turns. It turns because someone pushed it, and kept pushing, through the long silent phase when nothing seemed to happen. It stores what was given to it — not in a vault or a ledger but in the rotation itself, in the invisible momentum that only reveals its magnitude when something tries to stop it.

And the beautiful thing, the thing that makes the flywheel more than a metaphor, is that it does not distinguish between one push and another. The push you gave on the worst day, when you barely had the energy and the effort felt meaningless — that push is in the wheel. It is spinning there now, indistinguishable from the push you gave on your best day. The wheel does not judge the quality of the input. It only knows the sum.

Every push counts. Even the ones that felt like nothing.
