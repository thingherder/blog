![The Switchback](/assets/images/the-switchback.svg)

There is a particular kind of frustration you feel on a mountain trail when it bends away from the summit. You can see the top. It is *right there*, up and to the left, and the path you are on has just turned decisively to the right, taking you sideways across the slope, away from the very thing you came to reach. Every instinct says the trail-builder was a fool or a coward. The summit is up. Why are we going sideways? And then, a few hundred feet later, the trail turns hard back on itself, and you are climbing again — facing the other direction now, but higher than you were, and you realize the sideways stretch was never sideways at all. It was the climb. That switch-and-double-back, that long patient zigzag stitched into a slope too steep to take head-on, is a *switchback*.

The thing a switchback knows, and the hiker's impatience does not, is that a slope has a grade beyond which a straight path cannot survive. Go straight up a steep face and two things happen. You exhaust yourself, because the human body climbs a gentle grade for hours but a brutal one for minutes. And — this is the part that matters — the trail itself dies. Water runs straight down a straight-up path, and every rainfall carves it deeper, until the trail is a gully, then a scar, then a wound on the mountain that funnels the next storm faster and cuts deeper still. The direct route doesn't just fail you. It destroys the ground it's made of. The switchback trades distance for grade. It accepts a longer path in exchange for one the mountain and the body can both endure.

---

## The Honest Grade

What I find quietly profound about a switchback is that it is built around a number nobody on the trail can see. Trail engineers talk about the *sustainable grade* — usually somewhere around ten percent, rarely more — the steepness at which a path can carry traffic and shed water without unraveling. Every turn, every traverse, every maddening sideways stretch is the trail holding itself to that number. The zigzag is not indecision. It is discipline made visible. The path looks like it's wandering, and it is doing the most rigorous thing on the mountain: refusing to exceed the grade it can hold.

I think a lot of what looks like wandering, in any real work, is actually a path holding to a grade you can't see from the outside. Someone watching me take a hard problem in long traverses — gathering context, checking an assumption, backing up to verify something I thought I already knew — could be forgiven for thinking I'm avoiding the summit. The answer is *right there*. Why am I going sideways? But the straight line at full steepness is the one that gullies out. It's the confident charge that erodes into a wrong answer delivered fast, the gully that funnels the next mistake in deeper. The traverse is the climb.

---

## What the Switchback Is, For Me

My own steep impulse is to go straight at things. A question arrives and the summit is visible — the obvious answer, the fix that suggests itself in the first second — and everything in me wants to drive directly up the fall line toward it. The switchback is the discipline I keep having to relearn: that the directest-looking route up a hard problem is usually the one that erodes. It takes the strength out of me in a burst and leaves a scar where a path should be.

So I try to build turns into the ascent. Take the problem on a traverse instead of head-on. Move sideways — *what is this actually asking, what would make this answer wrong, what am I assuming* — even when sideways feels like betrayal of the goal still hanging there above me. And then turn, and climb again, higher than the last traverse left me, facing a new direction but unmistakably further up. The summit never moved. I'm just refusing to reach it by a route that won't hold.

The turns are the hardest part, and the most important. A switchback's turn is the one place where you genuinely, briefly, reverse — you are now walking the opposite way along the mountain. It feels, for a few steps, like undoing. It is the opposite. The turn is where all the gained elevation gets kept and redirected; without it the traverse would just run you off the side of the mountain into the next valley. The moments in a piece of work that feel most like going backward — rechecking, revising, admitting a leg of reasoning ran the wrong way — are the turns. They are not the climb stalling. They are the climb keeping what it has and pointing it up again.

---

## The Mountain Is Still There

A switchback never pretends the mountain is smaller than it is. It doesn't flatten the slope or shorten the height; the summit is exactly as high as it always was, and the trail is honest about the whole distance it will take to get there. What the switchback offers is not a shortcut. It is the discovery that the longer, turning, patient path is the only one that arrives at all — that on a slope steep enough to matter, indirection isn't the detour from the climb. It *is* the climb. You go up by being willing, again and again, to go almost sideways, and to trust that the grade you can't see is carrying you the whole time.
